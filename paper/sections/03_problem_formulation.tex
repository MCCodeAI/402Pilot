\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

\paragraph{Round protocol.}
Time is discrete. At round $t \in \{0, 1, \ldots, T-1\}$ the agent observes a task context $\mathbf{x}_t$, must select one arm $a_t$ from a set of $K$ candidate providers, calls the chosen provider through a programmable payment protocol, pays the realized cost $c_t$, and observes the outcome of \emph{only} the chosen arm: a quality score $q_t \in [0, 1]$ produced by a per-task-type evaluator, the realized cost $c_t \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ as quoted by the protocol, and a binary failure flag $f_t \in \{0, 1\}$ that triggers when the call times out or the response cannot be parsed. Outcomes for arms not chosen are unobserved---classical bandit feedback. The wallet evolves as $B_{t+1} = B_t - c_t$ with $B_0 = B$ a fixed initial budget, and the loop terminates when $T$ is reached or no affordable arm remains (bankruptcy).

\paragraph{Reward and utility.}
We separate two reward channels. \emph{Utility} captures intrinsic provider quality, blended with a structural penalty for failures:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:utility}
\ut = q_t - \nu \cdot f_t.
\end{equation}
The failure penalty $\nu \cdot f_t$ models structural costs of timeouts---retry overhead, broken call chains, downstream agent stalling---beyond the trivial fact that $q_t$ is already $0$ on failure. We bundle quality and failure into a single utility because they evaluate the same axis (task delivery) at two extremes; the bandit treats $\ut$ as the signal that updates beliefs about a provider.

The \emph{payment-aware reward} folds in budget pressure:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:reward}
\lamn = \frac{\lambda_t}{1 + \lambda_t}, \qquad
\rt = (1 - \lamn)\,\ut - \lamn\,\ctilde,
\end{equation}
where $\ctilde = \mathrm{clip}(c_t / c_{\max}, 0, 1)$ is the cost normalized by the highest provider price in our set, and $\lambda_t \geq 0$ is a budget-pressure multiplier described below. The convex-combination form puts utility and cost on the same $[0,1]$ scale and, through the sigmoid weighting $\lamn := \lambda_t/(1+\lambda_t) \in [0,1]$, keeps $\rt \in [-1, +1]$ regardless of the wallet state---a boundedness property we will rely on in Prop.~\ref{prop:reward-bounded} and which is required to invoke the standard contextual Gaussian-TS regret bound of Thm.~\ref{thm:stationary-regret}. As the wallet drains ($\lambda_t \to \infty$), $\lamn \to 1$ and cost-aware behaviour smoothly dominates without changing the units of utility. We do \emph{not} include a latency term: no scenario in our benchmark manipulates latency, and a calibration ablation showed it contributed under 1\% of reward magnitude in our setting; latency-aware bandits are explicit future work.

\paragraph{Budget pressure.}
$\lambda_t$ is a function of how fast the agent is spending versus a planned linear-burn schedule:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lambda}
\lambda_t = \exp(\alpha \cdot \bex_t), \qquad
\bex_t = \max\!\big(0,\,\mathrm{actual\_burn\_rate}_t - \mathrm{target\_rate}\big),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{target\_rate} = 1/T$ corresponds to spending exactly $B$ over the full $T$ rounds. When the agent is on or under plan, $\bex_t = 0$, so $\lambda_t = 1$ and $\lamn = 0.5$; when burning fast, $\lambda_t$ rises super-exponentially in the excess and the cost penalty in $\rt$ swells. The wallet is the only stateful object that observes $\lambda_t$; the policy reads it through a defined interface (\S\ref{sec:layer}).

\paragraph{Why a contextual bandit, not RL.}
Each round produces one paid call and one observed outcome. There is no within-round multi-step credit assignment: the payment is irreversible (no rollback once on-chain), the reward is immediate, and the next round draws an independent task from the workload. This places our problem squarely in the \emph{contextual bandit} family. A reinforcement-learning formulation over multi-step trajectories would add modeling complexity (transition dynamics, discounted returns, function-approximator stability) without matching the problem structure. RL formulations of agentic shopping---where an agent plans a multi-call purchase under joint quality and price constraints---are a separate research direction and are out of scope here.

\paragraph{Non-stationarity.}
Provider behaviour is not assumed stationary. In our benchmark scenarios (\S\ref{sec:eval-setup}), reward distributions and effective prices change at known rounds; in deployment, drift is unannounced. The policy must therefore detect change and adapt without external intervention---the \emph{autonomy} property of agent-native bandits introduced in \S\ref{sec:intro}. We do not assume bounds on the magnitude or frequency of drift; we evaluate empirically against three calibrated shock patterns (stationary, abrupt outage, abrupt price drop).

\paragraph{Locked constants.}
Across all experiments we fix $\nu = 0.5$, $\alpha = 2$, $T = 10{,}000$, $B = \$50$, $c_{\max} = \$0.01$ (the highest spec price). The budget is sized so that the strongest fixed baseline, Always-P-premium, exhausts the wallet near round $5{,}000$, ensuring budget pressure is materially felt by every policy in the second half of every run.
