\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A new family of programmable payment protocols---x402~\citep{coinbase2025x402v2}, AP2~\citep{google2025ap2}, A2A--x402~\citep{a2ax402}, A402~\citep{a402}---now lets autonomous software agents pay for online services at machine speed. An agent holding a stablecoin wallet can hit a paid endpoint, settle a per-call micropayment on-chain, and receive a response without a human in the loop. Per-call payments between autonomous agents and arbitrary services are no longer hypothetical; they are an operational substrate for machine-to-machine commerce. But protocol-level support for \emph{executing} payments answers only half of the buyer's problem. The other half---\emph{which} provider to pay, given a finite wallet, uncertain service quality, and prices and reliability that drift over time---the protocol stack does not answer. The x402 V2 specification surfaces machine-readable discovery metadata such as capability, price, and accepted payment options, but does not prescribe how a buyer should choose among the candidate endpoints, leaving provider selection as a buyer-side concern~\citep{coinbase2025x402v2}. What is missing is not a new protocol, but a \emph{buyer-side decision policy} that runs above the protocol stack and learns to spend a wallet under uncertainty.

Four threads of prior work are adjacent to this regime but do not cover it: classical bandits, cost-aware LLM routing, programmable payment protocols, and mechanism design over agent markets. Classical bandit theory supplies the relevant ingredients individually---budget constraints~\citep{xia2015bts,badanidiyuru2018bandits}, non-stationary forgetting~\citep{qi2023dsts}, and contextual value estimation~\citep{agrawal2013contextualts}---but always in settings where cost is a known scalar, the experiment terminates cleanly, and a human researcher operates the policy. Cost-aware LLM routing~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt,ong2024routellm,ding2024hybridllm,mixllm2024,llmbandit2025} addresses quality--cost tradeoffs across multiple models, but evaluation is offline against static rate cards, no payment protocol enters the decision loop, and prices do not change mid-run. Programmable payment protocols supply settlement, identity, and discovery, but explicitly leave provider choice to the buyer. Mechanism-design work on truthful reverse auctions over agent markets~\citep{aamas2026reverseauction} approaches provider selection from the opposite side---how a designer elicits truthful bids---rather than how a single buyer learns to spend a wallet under uncertainty.

We name this regime \emph{agent-native bandits}, distinguished from agent-implemented but human-operated bandits by three properties. First, the policy must remain coherent across the entire run without depending on a researcher to reset state, retune hyperparameters after every shock, or curate the provider pool between rounds---humans may still intervene in extreme cases, but routine operation cannot assume they will. Second, the agent owns and watches a wallet that drains: budget pressure is a state observed and acted on at every round, not a static cap that triggers shutdown. Third, costs are first-class observations; the agent reads a quoted price, pays it, and must learn to predict prices the same way it learns to predict quality, because both can change. Together these properties make the buyer's selection problem irreducibly online and economic: autonomous payment is not only a settlement problem, the buyer must learn both quality and price under wallet pressure, and the buyer-optimal allocation is therefore wallet-, context-, and shock-aware rather than any fixed-arm rule.

We instantiate this view in two pieces. \emph{402Pilot} is a protocol-agnostic decision layer that sits between an autonomous agent and the payment stack and commits to a clean separation: the protocol substrate handles how to pay (settlement, identity, discovery); 402Pilot handles which provider to pay (selection, learning, adaptation). Two stable interfaces between the agent and the layer make the policy pluggable, so any comparator we evaluate drops in without touching the rest of the stack. \emph{PA-DCT} (Payment-Aware Discounted Contextual Thompson sampling) is the policy we run inside the layer: a payment-aware Thompson-sampling rule that maintains discounted, contextual posteriors over both quality and cost per (provider, task-type) bucket, weighted at decision time by a sigmoid of wallet pressure. Treating cost as a learned posterior rather than a known scalar---inside payment-awareness, not bolted on---is the structural commitment that lets one policy detect price shocks and quality shocks symmetrically; the rest of the mechanism is deferred to \S\ref{sec:method}.

To evaluate this in the agent-native regime, we construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a reproducible replay benchmark of $823$ tasks across coding, multi-hop QA, and web search; five heterogeneous providers, including same-price adversarial and flaky variants whose differences emerge only through outcome feedback; and three calibrated market scenarios that probe stationary identity (S1), reliability shocks (S2), and price shocks (S3), each run for $30$ seeds and $10{,}000$ rounds per cell. On 402Pilot-Bench, PA-DCT pays only a small honest exploration cost in the stationary scenario; recovers from the mid-tier outage $35\%$ faster than its no-discount variant; and migrates spend onto a price-promoted high-quality provider within roughly $200$ rounds of the shock. PA-DCT Pareto-dominates every fixed-arm baseline on the joint quality--cost frontier in both non-stationary scenarios. An ablation over PA-DCT's four components, plus a diagnostic isolation of the cost-posterior pathway inside payment-awareness, shows that no single classical bandit metric reveals all of these contributions; we therefore report a five-metric framework (cumulative payment-aware reward, ROI, mean quality, regret, and adaptation time) and recommend it for future work in the regime.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We make three contributions. \textbf{C1.} We formalize \emph{agent-native bandits} and introduce \emph{402Pilot}, a protocol-agnostic buyer-side decision layer that separates provider selection from payment execution and instantiates above any quote-and-pay payment protocol (\S\ref{sec:layer}). \textbf{C2.} We propose \emph{PA-DCT}, a payment-aware Thompson-sampling policy whose learned cost posterior is the structural commitment that lets a single policy detect price shocks and quality shocks symmetrically (\S\ref{sec:method}). \textbf{C3.} We construct and release \emph{402Pilot-Bench} and use it to show that PA-DCT Pareto-dominates fixed-arm baselines on the joint quality--cost frontier in non-stationary scenarios while paying only a small honest exploration cost in the stationary one (\S\ref{sec:eval}).

\S\ref{sec:related-work} positions 402Pilot against four threads of prior work. \S\ref{sec:problem} formalizes the round protocol, reward, and budget dynamics. \S\ref{sec:layer} describes the decision-layer architecture and its interfaces. \S\ref{sec:method} gives PA-DCT in detail with pseudocode and theoretical properties. \S\ref{sec:eval} reports the main results, ablations, and limitations.
