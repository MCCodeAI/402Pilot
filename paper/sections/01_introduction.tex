\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Recent programmable payment protocols and proposals---including x402~\citep{coinbase2025x402v2}, AP2~\citep{google2025ap2}, A2A--x402~\citep{a2ax402}, and A402~\citep{a402}---make it increasingly practical for software agents to initiate and settle payments without an interactive checkout step. In a typical quote--pay--observe loop, a wallet-bearing agent requests a paid resource, receives machine-readable payment requirements or service metadata, submits a payment payload, and receives the response after verification. This protocol-level support addresses \emph{payment execution}: authorization, settlement, identity, discovery, and, in proposals such as A402, stronger binding between payment and service delivery. It does not by itself solve the buyer's prior decision: \emph{which} provider should the agent pay, given a finite wallet, uncertain service quality, and prices or reliability that may change during deployment? x402 V2, for example, adds automatic discovery and dynamic service metadata, including pricing information, but it does not prescribe a buyer-side ranking or selection policy~\citep{coinbase2025x402v2}. The missing component is therefore not another settlement protocol, but a \emph{buyer-side decision policy} that runs above the payment stack and learns how to spend a wallet under uncertainty.

Four bodies of work are adjacent to this regime but do not cover it. Classical bandit theory supplies important ingredients---budget constraints~\citep{xia2015bts,badanidiyuru2018bandits}, non-stationary forgetting~\citep{qi2023dsts}, and contextual value estimation~\citep{agrawal2013contextualts}---but these models are normally formulated outside a quote--pay--observe payment loop. Budgeted bandits may model rewards and costs jointly, yet the cost is a resource-consumption signal inside the learning problem rather than a market-facing quote that drains a wallet and may itself drift. Cost-aware LLM routing~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt,ong2024routellm,ding2024hybridllm,mixllm2024,llmbandit2025} studies quality--cost tradeoffs across models and APIs, including dynamic routing policies, but generally assumes a platform-controlled router and published price cards rather than a protocol-mediated payment decision with realized settlement costs. Programmable payment protocols supply the execution substrate but leave provider choice to the buyer-side application. Mechanism-design work on truthful reverse auctions over model providers~\citep{patra2026reverseauction} is complementary: it studies how a market designer can elicit truthful bids, whereas we study how a single autonomous buyer should adapt its own spending policy under sequential feedback.

We call this regime \emph{agent-native bandits}. It differs from agent-implemented but human-operated bandits in three ways. First, the policy is part of a long-running agent system: routine operation should not rely on a researcher to reset state, retune hyperparameters after each shock, or curate the provider pool between rounds. Second, the agent owns a wallet that drains over time, so budget pressure is an observed state that affects each decision rather than only a terminal constraint. Third, costs are first-class observations. The agent reads a quoted price, pays it, observes the delivered outcome, and must learn to predict prices as well as quality when either signal changes. Together, these properties make autonomous micropayment not only a settlement problem, but an online economic decision problem. A suitable buyer-side policy must therefore be wallet-aware, context-aware, and shock-aware, and it must learn these tradeoffs from feedback rather than from fixed provider labels.

We instantiate this view in two pieces. \emph{402Pilot} is a protocol-agnostic decision layer that sits between an autonomous agent and the payment stack. It enforces a clean separation: the protocol substrate handles how to pay---settlement, authorization, identity, and discovery---whereas 402Pilot handles which provider to pay---selection, learning, and adaptation. Two stable interfaces between the agent and the layer make the policy pluggable, so each comparator we evaluate can be exchanged without changing the rest of the stack. \emph{PA-DCT} (Payment-Aware Discounted Contextual Thompson Sampling) is the policy we run inside the layer. It maintains discounted, contextual posteriors over both quality and cost for each provider--task-type bucket, and combines posterior samples at decision time using a wallet-pressure weight. Treating realized cost as a learned posterior rather than a fixed scalar, inside the payment-aware objective, is the structural commitment that lets one policy respond to both reliability shocks and price shocks. The full mechanism is given in \S\ref{sec:method}.

To evaluate this regime, we construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a reproducible replay benchmark with $823$ tasks across coding, multi-hop QA, and web search; five heterogeneous providers, including same-price adversarial and flaky variants whose differences emerge only through outcome feedback; and three calibrated market scenarios that test stationarity (S1), reliability shocks (S2), and price shocks (S3). Each policy--scenario cell is run for $30$ seeds and $10{,}000$ rounds. On 402Pilot-Bench, PA-DCT incurs a measured exploration cost in the stationary scenario, where adaptation provides no benefit; recovers from the mid-tier outage $35\%$ faster than its no-discount variant; and shifts spend to a price-promoted high-quality provider within roughly $200$ rounds of the shock. In the non-stationary scenarios, PA-DCT wins $13$ of $14$ baseline comparisons on cumulative payment-aware reward; the remaining comparison is a statistically non-significant tie against the cheapest fixed arm in S2, where PA-DCT delivers substantially higher mean quality. Ablations over PA-DCT's components, together with a diagnostic isolation of the learned cost-posterior pathway, show that no single classical bandit metric captures all relevant behavior. We therefore report a five-metric evaluation framework: cumulative payment-aware reward, ROI, mean quality, regret, and adaptation time.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We make three contributions.

\textbf{C1.} We formalize \emph{agent-native bandits}, a sequential decision regime induced by autonomous quote--pay--observe interactions, and introduce \emph{402Pilot}, a protocol-agnostic buyer-side decision layer that separates provider selection from payment execution (\S\ref{sec:layer}).

\textbf{C2.} We propose \emph{PA-DCT}, a payment-aware discounted contextual Thompson-sampling policy that learns posteriors over both quality and cost, allowing a single policy to adapt to reliability shocks and price shocks within the same decision rule (\S\ref{sec:method}).

\textbf{C3.} We construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a reproducible benchmark for agent micropayment selection, and evaluate policies under stationary, reliability-shock, and price-shock scenarios using a five-metric framework that captures reward, cost efficiency, quality, regret, and adaptation speed (\S\ref{sec:eval}).

\S\ref{sec:related-work} positions 402Pilot against four threads of prior work. \S\ref{sec:problem} formalizes the round protocol, reward, and budget dynamics. \S\ref{sec:layer} describes the decision-layer architecture and its interfaces. \S\ref{sec:method} gives PA-DCT in detail with pseudocode and theoretical properties. \S\ref{sec:eval} reports the main results, ablations, and limitations.
