\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

x402 makes HTTP-native payments available to software agents: an agent can request a paid endpoint, receive machine-readable payment requirements, submit a payment payload, and obtain the response without an interactive checkout step. x402 V2 further adds discovery and dynamic service metadata, including pricing information, so that candidate paid services can be surfaced programmatically~\citep{coinbase2025x402v2}. This protocol support addresses \emph{payment execution}: how a payment is requested, authorized, settled, and verified. It does not by itself answer the buyer's prior decision: \emph{which} provider should the agent pay, given a finite wallet, uncertain service quality, and a market in which prices and reliability may change? The missing component is therefore not another settlement mechanism, but a \emph{buyer-side decision policy} that runs above x402 and learns how to spend a wallet under uncertainty.

Several lines of prior work supply pieces of this problem, but not the full buyer-side setting. Budgeted bandits model resource-constrained learning~\citep{xia2015bts,badanidiyuru2018bandits}; discounted methods address non-stationarity~\citep{qi2023dsts}; and contextual Thompson sampling estimates task-conditioned value~\citep{agrawal2013contextualts}. These ingredients are usually analyzed separately, or without the protocol-mediated buyer who must choose after observing payable endpoints and then update from realized prices and service outcomes. Cost-aware LLM routing~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt,ong2024routellm,ding2024hybridllm,mixllm2024,llmbandit2025} is closer operationally, but it typically treats prices as published rate-card inputs rather than learned market signals in a draining-wallet payment loop. Conversely, payment protocols expose the execution substrate, and auction mechanisms study strategic provider bidding~\citep{patra2026reverseauction}; neither provides an online spending policy for a single autonomous buyer. To our knowledge, existing work does not combine finite-wallet budget pressure, task context, non-stationary adaptation, and learned realized cost in one buyer-side policy for programmable micropayments.

We call this regime \emph{agent-native bandits}. It differs from agent-implemented but human-operated bandits in three ways. First, the policy must run as part of an autonomous system, so routine operation cannot depend on a researcher resetting state, retuning parameters, or curating providers after every change. Second, the agent spends from a wallet that keeps draining, making budget pressure an evolving state rather than a static terminal constraint. Third, the market is non-stationary: both service quality and effective price can shift, and the policy must adapt from feedback after payments have already been made. Because each paid call is irreversible and selects only one provider, the agent cannot try every candidate in a round and keep only the best response. These properties make autonomous micropayment an online economic decision problem, not only a settlement problem. A suitable buyer-side policy must therefore be autonomous, wallet-aware, context-aware, and responsive to market drift.

This paper proposes \emph{402Pilot}, a protocol-agnostic decision layer that separates provider selection from payment execution. The payment stack handles how to pay; 402Pilot decides which provider to pay and updates that decision rule from post-payment feedback. Inside the layer we introduce \emph{PA-DCT} (Payment-Aware Discounted Contextual Thompson Sampling), a policy that combines wallet pressure, task context, discounted learning, and posterior estimates of both quality and realized cost. This combination lets the same policy react to reliability shocks and price shocks without hard-coding provider labels or assuming fixed prices.

To evaluate this regime, we construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a reproducible replay benchmark with $823$ tasks, five heterogeneous providers, and three market scenarios: stationary operation, a reliability shock, and a price shock. Across $30$ seeds and $10{,}000$ rounds per policy--scenario cell, PA-DCT pays a measured exploration cost in the stationary case but adapts under both non-stationary scenarios: it recovers from the reliability shock $35\%$ faster than its no-discount variant and detects the price promotion at the first measurable adaptation window, $200$ rounds into a $9{,}000$-round post-shock horizon. Overall, PA-DCT wins $13$ of $14$ non-stationary baseline comparisons on cumulative payment-aware reward; the remaining comparison is a statistically non-significant tie against the cheapest fixed arm, where PA-DCT delivers substantially higher mean quality. The ablations show that reward, cost efficiency, quality, regret, and adaptation speed are all needed to characterize behavior in this setting.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
We make three contributions.

\textbf{C1.} We introduce and formalize \emph{agent-native bandits}---to our knowledge, the first academic formulation of the quote--pay--observe bandit regime induced by autonomous programmable payments---and propose \emph{402Pilot}, a protocol-agnostic buyer-side decision layer that separates provider selection from payment execution (\S\ref{sec:layer}).

\textbf{C2.} We propose \emph{PA-DCT}, a payment-aware discounted contextual Thompson-sampling policy that learns posteriors over both quality and cost, allowing a single policy to adapt to reliability shocks and price shocks within the same decision rule (\S\ref{sec:method}).

\textbf{C3.} We construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a reproducible benchmark for agent micropayment selection that isolates hidden provider quality, reliability shocks, and price shocks, enabling controlled evaluation of reward, cost efficiency, quality, regret, and adaptation speed in the same testbed (\S\ref{sec:eval}).

\S\ref{sec:related-work} positions 402Pilot against four threads of prior work. \S\ref{sec:problem} formalizes the round protocol, reward, and budget dynamics. \S\ref{sec:layer} describes the decision-layer architecture and its interfaces. \S\ref{sec:method} gives PA-DCT in detail with pseudocode and theoretical properties. \S\ref{sec:eval} reports the main results, ablations, and limitations.
