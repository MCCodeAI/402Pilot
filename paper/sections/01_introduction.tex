\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A new family of programmable payment protocols---x402~\citep{coinbase2025x402v2}, AP2~\citep{google2025ap2}, A2A--x402~\citep{a2ax402}, A402~\citep{a402}---now lets autonomous software agents pay for online services at machine speed. An agent holding a stablecoin wallet can hit a paid endpoint, settle a per-call micropayment on-chain, and receive a response without a human in the loop. Per-call payments between autonomous agents and arbitrary services are no longer hypothetical; they are an operational substrate for machine-to-machine commerce. But protocol-level support for \emph{executing} payments answers only half of the buyer's problem. The other half---\emph{which} provider to pay, given a finite wallet, uncertain service quality, and prices and reliability that drift over time---the protocol stack does not answer. The x402 V2 specification recognizes this gap explicitly and leaves provider selection to the buyer ``based on historical outcomes''~\citep{coinbase2025x402v2}, without specifying the policy itself. What is missing is not a new protocol, but a \emph{buyer-side decision policy} that runs above the protocol stack and learns to spend a wallet under uncertainty.

Four bodies of prior work touch this problem without covering it. Classical bandit theory provides the right ingredients individually---budget constraints~\citep{xia2015bts,badanidiyuru2018bandits}, non-stationary forgetting~\citep{qi2023dsts}, and contextual value estimation~\citep{agrawal2013contextualts}---but in settings where cost is a known scalar, an experiment terminates cleanly, and a human researcher operates the policy. Cost-aware LLM routing~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt,ong2024routellm,ding2024hybridllm,mixllm2024,llmbandit2025} addresses quality--cost tradeoffs over multiple models, but evaluation runs offline against static rate cards, no payment protocol enters the decision loop, and prices do not change mid-run. Programmable payment protocols deliver settlement, identity, and discovery, but they explicitly leave provider choice to the buyer. Mechanism-design work on truthful reverse auctions over agent markets~\citep{aamas2026reverseauction} approaches provider selection from the opposite side---how a designer should elicit truthful bids---rather than how a single buyer should learn to spend. None of these threads is the regime an autonomous buyer actually operates in.

We name this regime \emph{agent-native bandits}, distinguished from agent-implemented but human-operated bandits by three properties. First, the operator is the autonomous agent itself: there is no human team that resets state, retunes hyperparameters after a price shock, or removes a degraded provider; the policy must remain coherent across the entire run. Second, the agent owns and watches a wallet that drains; budget pressure is a state observed and acted on at every round, not a static cap that triggers shutdown. Third, costs are first-class observations: the agent reads a quoted price, pays it, and must learn to predict prices the same way it learns to predict quality, because both can change. Naming the regime is consequential because the central claim of this paper follows from it: \textbf{autonomous payment is not only a settlement problem; it is an online economic decision problem in which the buyer must learn both service quality and price simultaneously.} The buyer-optimal policy in this regime is therefore not Always-cheap, Always-premium, or any fixed-arm rule---it is a wallet-, context-, and shock-aware allocation that no static rule reproduces and that must be learned from feedback alone.

We instantiate this view in two pieces. \textbf{402Pilot} is a protocol-agnostic decision layer that sits between an autonomous agent and the payment stack and commits to a clean separation: the protocol substrate handles \emph{how} to pay (settlement, identity, discovery); 402Pilot handles \emph{which} provider to pay (selection, learning, adaptation). Two stable interfaces---\texttt{select(task, affordable\_providers)} and \texttt{update(task, provider, utility, observed\_cost)}---are the only points of contact, and every comparator we evaluate (Random, three Always-X variants, BudgetRule, PA-DCT) implements them uniformly. \textbf{PA-DCT} (Payment-Aware Discounted Contextual Thompson sampling) is the policy we run inside the layer. It carries four classical bandit components into the agent-native regime: (1) \textbf{Payment-aware} ranking, where decision-time scores combine a sampled quality posterior with a \emph{learned cost posterior} weighted by a sigmoid of wallet pressure---this is what makes price shocks and quality shocks symmetric inputs to the same policy, the structural commitment that distinguishes PA-DCT from prior payment-aware bandit work; (2) \textbf{Discounted} sufficient statistics, so observations from before a market shift weigh less than recent ones; (3) \textbf{Contextual} bucketing, with a separate posterior per (provider, task-type) cell so that ``provider $a$ is good at coding but weak on multi-hop QA'' is learnable; and (4) \textbf{Thompson sampling}, so exploration is driven by posterior uncertainty rather than by an externally-tuned schedule.

To evaluate this in the agent-native regime, we construct \textbf{402Pilot-Bench}, a reproducible replay benchmark: $823$ tasks across coding (HumanEval), multi-hop QA (HotpotQA), and web search (TriviaQA closed-form plus a custom open-ended subset, evaluated under deterministic and LLM-as-judge scorers respectively); five heterogeneous providers including an \emph{adversarial} provider that returns fluent-but-wrong answers and a \emph{flaky} provider that times out $40\%$ of calls---both at the same nominal price tier as the reliable mid-tier provider, so no static rule can tell them apart from outcome feedback alone; three calibrated market scenarios (stationary, mid-tier outage, premium price promotion); $30$ seeds $\times$ $10{,}000$ rounds per cell, totaling $20{,}575$ pre-generated frozen LLM responses. On 402Pilot-Bench, PA-DCT recovers from the mid-tier outage $35\%$ faster than its no-discount variant, migrates spend onto the price-promoted P-premium within $\approx 200$ rounds of the round-$1{,}000$ shock, and Pareto-dominates every fixed-arm baseline on the joint (cumulative payment-aware reward, mean quality) frontier in both non-stationary scenarios while paying only a small honest exploration cost when the market is stationary. An ablation over PA-DCT's four components, plus a diagnostic isolation of the cost-posterior pathway inside payment-awareness, shows that no single classical bandit metric reveals all of these contributions: payment-awareness is essential to cumulative reward, the discount earns its keep on adaptation time, contextual bucketing helps where shock structure is task-type heterogeneous, Thompson sampling reduces seed variance $5$--$9\times$ and makes shock detection routine, and the cost posterior is what turns a price drop into an actionable signal. We report all five economic metrics (cum-PA, ROI, $\bar q$, regret, and adaptation time) and recommend a multi-metric framework for future work in this regime.

\paragraph{Contributions.}

\textbf{C1. Setting + decision layer.} We formalize \emph{agent-native bandits} and introduce \emph{402Pilot}, a protocol-agnostic buyer-side decision layer with two stable interfaces between agent decision-making and payment execution. The layer instantiates above x402 in this paper and applies unchanged to AP2, A2A--x402, A402, or any future quote-and-pay protocol; it never crosses the settlement boundary (\S\ref{sec:layer}).

\textbf{C2. PA-DCT algorithm.} We assemble a payment-aware Thompson-sampling rule for the agent-native regime: discounted dual posteriors over both quality and cost per (provider, task-type) bucket, decision-time wallet weighting, and Thompson exploration. Treating cost as a learned posterior rather than a known scalar inside payment-awareness is the structural commitment that lets one policy detect price shocks and quality shocks symmetrically (\S\ref{sec:method}).

\textbf{C3. 402Pilot-Bench and empirical findings.} We construct and release a reproducible benchmark---$823$ tasks $\times$ $5$ providers (including same-price adversarial and flaky variants) $\times$ $3$ scenarios $\times$ $30$ seeds $\times$ $10{,}000$ rounds; $20{,}575$ frozen responses---and use it to measure PA-DCT against five baselines. PA-DCT recovers $35\%$ faster than its no-discount variant under a mid-tier outage, adapts within $\approx\!200$ rounds to a premium price promotion, and Pareto-dominates every fixed-arm baseline on (cum-PA, $\bar q$) in both non-stationary scenarios. An ablation isolates each PA-DCT component on its own evaluation axis and motivates a five-metric framework (cum-PA, ROI, $\bar q$, regret, AdaptT) for the agent-native regime (\S\ref{sec:eval}).
