\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Protocols such as x402 make per-request software-agent payments programmable. An agent can request a paid endpoint, receive machine-readable payment requirements, submit a payment payload, and obtain the response without an interactive checkout step; x402 V2 further adds discovery and dynamic service metadata, including pricing information, so candidate paid services can be surfaced programmatically~\citep{coinbase2025x402v2}. This protocol support addresses \emph{payment execution}: how a selected payment is requested, authorized, settled, and verified. It does not by itself answer the buyer's prior decision: which service should an agent with a finite wallet pay for when quality is uncertain and prices or reliability may change? The missing component is therefore not another settlement mechanism, but a \emph{buyer-side decision policy} above payment execution that learns which services are worth buying under uncertainty.

Several lines of prior work supply pieces of this problem, but not the full buyer-side setting. Budgeted bandits model resource-constrained learning~\citep{xia2015bts,badanidiyuru2018bandits}; discounted methods address non-stationarity~\citep{qi2023dsts}; and contextual Thompson sampling estimates task-conditioned value~\citep{agrawal2013contextualts}. These ingredients are usually analyzed separately, outside a protocol-mediated quote--pay--observe loop in which the buyer sees payable endpoints, pays one of them, and then updates from realized prices and service outcomes. Cost-aware LLM routing~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt,ong2024routellm,ding2024hybridllm,mixllm2024,llmbandit2025} is closer operationally, but it typically treats prices as published rate-card inputs rather than feedback learned in a draining-wallet payment loop. Conversely, payment protocols expose the execution substrate, and auction mechanisms study strategic provider bidding~\citep{patra2026reverseauction}; neither provides an online spending policy for a single autonomous buyer. Existing work therefore leaves open the combination needed for agent-operated micropayments: finite-wallet budget pressure, task context, non-stationary adaptation, and learned realized cost.

We call this \emph{agent-native payment decision-making}. It differs from human-operated provider selection in three ways. First, the policy is part of an autonomous system, so routine operation cannot rely on a human resetting state, retuning parameters, or curating providers after each change. Second, the wallet drains with every paid call; each payment is irreversible and reveals only the chosen provider's outcome, so the agent cannot sample every candidate and keep the best response. Third, market conditions change: service quality, reliability, and effective price can shift while the policy observes them only through post-payment feedback. These properties make autonomous micropayment an online economic decision problem, not only a settlement problem.

To address this decision problem, this paper proposes \emph{402Pilot}, a protocol-agnostic layer that separates provider selection from payment execution. The payment stack handles how to pay; 402Pilot decides which service is worth buying and updates that decision rule from post-payment feedback. We instantiate the layer with \emph{PA-DCT} (Payment-Aware Discounted Contextual Thompson Sampling), a learning policy that maintains a Q-posterior over service utility and a separate posterior over realized cost for each provider--task bucket. Its ranking rule combines wallet pressure, task context, discounted updating, and posterior sampling, allowing the same policy to react to reliability shocks and price shocks without hard-coding provider labels or assuming fixed prices.

To evaluate this regime, we construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a reproducible replay benchmark with $823$ tasks, five heterogeneous providers, and three market scenarios: stationary operation, a reliability shock, and a price shock. Across $30$ seeds and $10{,}000$ rounds per policy--scenario cell, PA-DCT pays a measured exploration cost in the stationary setting but provides the most robust adaptive trade-off among tested non-oracle policies under non-stationarity. During the S2 outage, it improves full-horizon mean quality by $+15$ percentage points over the cheapest fixed provider while remaining budget-feasible; under the S3 price promotion, it improves over Always-P-mid on quality, ROI, and PA-gap/$T$. Ablations isolate the roles of payment-aware ranking, discounting, contextual buckets, Thompson sampling, and realized-cost learning: payment-awareness protects solvency, discounting and cost learning drive shock response, and context plus posterior sampling reduce brittle behavior across task types.

We make three contributions.

\textbf{C1.} We formulate \emph{agent-native payment decision-making} and introduce \emph{402Pilot}, a protocol-agnostic buyer-side decision layer for this setting. The formulation captures autonomous quote--pay--observe provider selection under a draining wallet and a drifting market; the layer supplies the missing provider-selection policy above payment execution (\S\ref{sec:layer}).

\textbf{C2.} We instantiate 402Pilot with \emph{PA-DCT}, a payment-aware discounted contextual Thompson-sampling policy with a Q-posterior over service utility and a separate posterior over realized cost, allowing the decision layer to adapt to reliability shocks and price shocks within the same rule (\S\ref{sec:method}).

\textbf{C3.} We construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a frozen-replay benchmark and evaluation protocol for agent micropayments. It decouples provider-output generation from policy evaluation, models hidden provider quality, reliability shocks, and price shocks, and provides a reusable multi-metric protocol for future work (\S\ref{sec:eval}).

\S\ref{sec:related-work} positions 402Pilot against payment protocols, bandit learning, and LLM provider-selection markets. \S\ref{sec:problem} formalizes the round protocol, reward, and budget dynamics. \S\ref{sec:layer} describes the decision-layer architecture and its interfaces. \S\ref{sec:method} gives PA-DCT in detail with pseudocode and theoretical properties. \S\ref{sec:eval} reports the main results, ablations, and limitations.
