\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The rapid development of x402 brings protocol capabilities that traditional payment flows do not support: machine-readable payment requirements, per-request micropayments, and low-friction settlement. With further advances such as x402 V2~\citep{coinbase2025x402v2}, additional capabilities---service discovery, dynamic service metadata, and pricing---are exposed as programmatic interfaces. These capabilities make agent micropayment programmatically executable, and naturally raise the question studied in this paper: how should an autonomous agent with a finite wallet decide which service is worth buying in a changing market?

Several lines of prior work supply pieces of this problem, but not the full buyer-side setting. Budgeted bandits model resource-constrained learning~\citep{xia2015bts,badanidiyuru2018bandits}; discounted methods address non-stationarity~\citep{qi2023dsts}; and contextual Thompson sampling estimates task-conditioned value~\citep{agrawal2013contextualts}. These ingredients are usually analyzed separately, outside a protocol-mediated quote--pay--observe loop in which the buyer sees payable endpoints, pays one of them, and then updates from realized prices and service outcomes. Cost-aware LLM routing~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt,ong2024routellm,ding2024hybridllm,mixllm2024,llmbandit2025} is closer operationally, but it typically treats prices as published rate-card inputs rather than feedback learned in a draining-wallet payment loop. Conversely, payment protocols expose the execution substrate, and auction mechanisms study strategic provider bidding~\citep{patra2026reverseauction}; neither provides an online spending policy for a single autonomous buyer. Existing work therefore leaves open the combination needed for agent-operated micropayments: finite-wallet budget pressure, task context, non-stationary adaptation, and learned realized cost.

We call this \emph{agent-native payment decision-making}. It differs from human-operated provider selection in three ways. First, the policy is part of an autonomous system, so routine operation cannot rely on a human resetting state, retuning parameters, or curating providers after each change. Second, the wallet drains with every paid call; each payment is irreversible and reveals only the chosen provider's outcome, so the agent cannot sample every candidate and keep the best response. Third, market conditions change: service quality, reliability, and effective price can shift while the policy observes them only through post-payment feedback. These properties make autonomous micropayment an online economic decision problem, not only a settlement problem.

To address this decision problem, this paper proposes \emph{402Pilot}, a protocol-agnostic layer that separates provider selection from payment execution. The payment stack handles how to pay; 402Pilot decides which service is worth buying and updates that decision rule from post-payment feedback. We instantiate the layer with \emph{PA-DCT} (Payment-Aware Discounted Contextual Thompson Sampling), a learning policy that maintains two discount-tracked posteriors for each provider--task bucket: a Q-posterior over service utility and a separate posterior over realized cost. Its ranking rule combines wallet pressure, task context, discounted updating, and posterior sampling, allowing the same policy to react to reliability drift and price drift without hard-coding provider labels or assuming fixed prices.

To evaluate this regime, we construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a reproducible replay benchmark with $823$ tasks, five heterogeneous provider pipelines, and three market scenarios: stationary operation, a reliability shock, and a price shock. Across $30$ seeds and $10{,}000$ rounds per policy--scenario cell, PA-DCT pays a measured exploration cost in the stationary setting but provides the most robust adaptive trade-off in the main non-oracle comparison under non-stationarity. During the S2 outage, it improves full-horizon mean quality by $+15$ percentage points over the cheapest fixed provider while remaining budget-feasible; under the S3 price promotion, it improves over Always-P-mid on quality, ROI, and PA-gap/$T$. Ablations isolate the roles of payment-aware ranking, discounting, contextual buckets, Thompson sampling, and realized-cost learning: payment-awareness protects solvency, discounting and cost learning drive shock response, and context plus posterior sampling reduce brittle behavior across task types.

We make three contributions.

\textbf{C1.} We formulate \emph{agent-native payment decision-making} and introduce \emph{402Pilot}, a protocol-agnostic buyer-side decision layer for this setting. The formulation captures autonomous quote--pay--observe provider selection under a draining wallet and a drifting market; the layer supplies the missing provider-selection policy above payment execution (\S\ref{sec:problem}--\S\ref{sec:layer}).

\textbf{C2.} We instantiate 402Pilot with \emph{PA-DCT}, a payment-aware discounted contextual Thompson-sampling policy with two discount-tracked posteriors: a Q-posterior over service utility and a separate posterior over realized cost, allowing the decision layer to adapt to reliability shocks and price shocks within the same rule (\S\ref{sec:method}).

\textbf{C3.} We construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a frozen-replay benchmark and evaluation protocol for agent micropayments. It decouples provider-output generation from policy evaluation, models hidden provider quality, reliability shocks, and price shocks, and provides a reusable multi-metric protocol for future work (\S\ref{sec:eval}).

\S\ref{sec:related-work} positions 402Pilot against payment protocols, bandit learning, and LLM provider-selection markets. \S\ref{sec:problem} formalizes the round protocol, reward, and budget dynamics. \S\ref{sec:layer} describes the decision-layer architecture and its interfaces. \S\ref{sec:method} gives PA-DCT in detail with pseudocode and theoretical properties. \S\ref{sec:eval} reports the main results, ablations, and limitations.
