\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discuss}

The future of agent micropayment is arriving quickly. Protocols are making payment execution programmatic, while complementary infrastructure for reputation, risk scoring, service attestations, and escrow-like settlement is beginning to emerge. These market-level mechanisms will be important, but they are not yet mature or broadly trusted. 402Pilot takes the buyer-side perspective in this interim: it asks what a user-controlled agent can do locally, with its own wallet and observations, to decide whether a quoted service is worth paying for. This decision layer is complementary to future third-party trust platforms, much as human buyers may consult merchant ratings while still making their own purchasing decisions. We hope this framing encourages broader work toward micropayment agents that can act for users reliably, autonomously, and intelligently.

The main limitation is that our quantitative evaluation is deliberately controlled. 402Pilot-Bench uses frozen replay rather than live x402 calls so that provider outputs, seeds, costs, and non-stationary scenarios remain reproducible across policies. The local x402 witness in Appendix~\ref{appendix:x402-witness} validates that the payment-execution boundary can be instantiated on a protocol-shaped local stack, but it does not measure live settlement latency, facilitator failures, congestion, or provider drift in deployment. A second limitation is market coverage: the benchmark covers a finite set of task types, providers, and scripted market changes, so performance under broader tasks, more open-ended demand, unexpected price or reliability shifts, strategic providers, shared-wallet contention, and external trust signals remains future work. Finally, quality feedback is only as strong as the scorer or judge that supplies it, especially for open-ended tasks such as T3b; stronger verification, broader scenario sweeps, and tighter non-stationary theory are natural next steps.
