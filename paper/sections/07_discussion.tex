\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discuss}

\paragraph{Decision and settlement are complementary.}
402Pilot is a pre-payment decision layer: it chooses which provider an agent should buy from before the HTTP request is issued. Protocols such as A402's atomic service channels~\citep{a402} address a different layer: delivery, dispute resolution, and atomic refund after a provider is selected. These mechanisms are therefore complementary. A settlement protocol can make the payment safer, but it does not decide whether a budgeted buyer should choose the cheap, reliable, premium, flaky, or temporarily discounted provider; that is the learning problem 402Pilot studies.

\paragraph{What the benchmark establishes.}
402Pilot-Bench is a controlled replay benchmark, not a live-market replica. Freezing $20{,}575$ pre-generated responses makes paired comparisons and ablations reproducible, and the calibrated provider set lets us isolate same-price quality differences, reliability shocks, and price shocks. The trade-off is external validity: the replay cannot capture provider drift outside the sampled response versions, facilitator latency, transaction-fee jitter, or live settlement failures. The next empirical step is therefore a rolling deployment over a real x402 facilitator, where both provider outputs and payment conditions can change online.

\paragraph{Measurement boundaries.}
The main evaluator caveat is the open-ended T3b bucket. Its cached LLM-as-judge score can accept fluent-but-wrong P-adv outputs more often than deterministic graders do, so adversarial-provider detection on T3b should be read as evaluator-conditioned rather than absolute truthfulness detection. The four task buckets are also intentionally coarse: they test whether task type is useful context, but specialized providers or narrower user intents may require richer context features or learned task embeddings.

\paragraph{Economic and deployment scope.}
The paper studies one buyer, one wallet, and non-strategic posted prices. It does not model shared reserves across concurrent agents, provider rate limits, partial fills under load, strategic price manipulation, or auction mechanisms such as those studied by~\citet{patra2026reverseauction}. A 402Pilot-style layer could consume richer quotes, attestations, or auction allocations as inputs, but those market-design questions are outside the present evaluation.

\paragraph{Algorithmic scope.}
PA-DCT uses fixed hyperparameters, including a fixed discount $\gamma$, and falls back to specification priors for cold-start providers. Explicit change-point detection, adaptive discounts, transfer priors, and policy or regulatory constraints over providers are natural extensions. On the theory side, our clean regret statement is the stationary, no-discount, policy-state result; the discounted analysis explains posterior adaptation after observations rather than a full coupled regret theorem for every piecewise-stationary wallet trajectory. Appendix~\ref{app:proof-limitations} records these technical boundaries. Within that scope, the results support the main claim: programmable settlement creates an online buyer-side decision problem over quality, price, and budget pressure, not only a payment-execution problem.
