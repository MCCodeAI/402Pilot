Programmable-payment protocols such as x402 let agents execute per-request payments, but they do not decide which service a budgeted agent should buy. We formalize this missing layer as \emph{agent-native payment decision-making}: contextual provider selection under a draining wallet, uncertain service quality, and non-stationary reliability or prices. We propose \emph{402Pilot}, a protocol-agnostic buyer-side decision layer, and \emph{PA-DCT}, a payment-aware discounted contextual Thompson-sampling policy for that layer. PA-DCT maintains posteriors over both service quality and realized cost for each provider--task bucket, so price changes and quality changes are both learned from post-payment feedback. We evaluate on \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a frozen-replay benchmark with $823$ tasks, five LLM providers, three market scenarios, and $30$ seeds $\times$ $10{,}000$ rounds per policy--scenario cell. PA-DCT pays an exploration cost in the stationary setting, but under non-stationarity it is the only non-oracle policy that learns from both reliability and price feedback: during a mid-tier outage it raises full-horizon mean quality by $+15$ percentage points over the cheapest fixed provider while remaining budget-feasible, and under a premium price promotion it improves over the reliable mid-tier baseline on quality, quality per dollar, and per-round PA-regret to the replay oracle. Ablations show that PA-DCT's components are exposed on different axes: solvency, shock response, task-dependent routing, exploration stability, and realized-price adaptation. These results show that agent payments require buyer-side learning over quality, cost, and budget pressure, not only payment execution.
