Programmable-payment protocols such as x402 let agents execute per-request payments, but they do not decide which service a budgeted agent should buy. We study this missing buyer-side layer as \emph{agent-native payment decision-making}: contextual provider selection under a draining wallet, uncertain service outcomes, and non-stationary reliability or prices. We propose \emph{402Pilot}, a protocol-agnostic decision layer above payment execution, and instantiate it with \emph{PA-DCT}, a payment-aware discounted contextual Thompson-sampling policy. PA-DCT maintains two discount-tracked posteriors for each provider--task bucket: a Q-posterior over service utility and a separate posterior over realized cost, letting both reliability drift and price drift be tracked from post-payment feedback within a single update rule. We evaluate on \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a frozen-replay benchmark with $823$ tasks, five LLM providers, three market scenarios, and $30$ seeds $\times$ $10{,}000$ rounds per policy--scenario cell. PA-DCT pays an exploration cost in the stationary setting, but among tested non-oracle policies it gives the most robust adaptive trade-off under non-stationarity: during a mid-tier outage it improves full-horizon mean quality by $+15$ percentage points over the cheapest fixed provider while remaining budget-feasible, and under a premium price promotion it improves over the reliable mid-tier baseline on quality, ROI, and PA-gap/$T$ to the replay oracle. Ablations isolate the roles of payment-aware ranking, discounting, contextual buckets, Thompson sampling, and realized-cost learning. 402Pilot therefore supplies the missing pilot for programmable payments: not another way to pay, but a learned policy that helps a budgeted agent decide which services are worth buying as market conditions change.
