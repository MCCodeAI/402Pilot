\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

There is no off-the-shelf benchmark for buyer-side payment decisions by autonomous agents: existing task datasets measure answer quality, but not repeated provider choice under a finite wallet, hidden provider reliability, realized prices, and post-payment feedback. We therefore build the evaluation from public task sources while making the paid-service interaction explicit. The key design is frozen replay: we generate multiple LLM responses for each task--provider pair once, score them once, and then replay those scored outcomes to every policy under shared seeds. This preserves variation from real LLM calls while making policy comparisons reproducible.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:eval-setup}

\paragraph{Benchmark.}
\emph{402Pilot-Bench} contains $823$ tasks across coding (HumanEval pass@1), multi-hop QA (HotpotQA max EM/F1), and web search. Web search is split into closed-form T3a (deterministic max EM/F1) and open-ended T3b (cached LLM-as-judge), yielding the four task buckets T1/T2/T3a/T3b used by PA-DCT ($K_b=4$). For each (task, provider) pair we pre-generate $V=5$ response versions, yielding $823\times5\times5=20{,}575$ scored responses. During evaluation, all policies see the same replay stream; scenario changes modify realized failures or effective prices, not the underlying task pool.

\paragraph{Providers.}
The provider set is small but structured. P-cheap, P-mid, and P-premium form the visible price--quality ladder that a budgeted buyer would expect. P-mid, P-adv, and P-flaky form the harder case: they share the same base price (\$0.002/call) and base model, but differ in observed outcomes. P-adv produces fluent but wrong answers, while P-flaky returns billed timeouts. A selector that only reads static metadata cannot separate these three arms; it has to learn from paid feedback. Table~\ref{tab:providers} summarizes the five pipelines.

\paragraph{Scenarios and scale.}
The same benchmark is then replayed under three market regimes. S1 is stationary operation. S2 injects a reliability shock: P-mid has $30\%$ forced failures from rounds $3{,}000$--$5{,}500$ and then recovers. S3 injects a price shock: at round $1{,}000$, P-premium's effective price drops from \$0.01 to \$0.002, matching the mid-tier price. Each (policy, scenario) cell uses $30$ seeds, $T=10{,}000$ rounds, and budget $B=\$50$. At the listed premium price, the budget buys only $5{,}000$ premium calls, so a policy must manage wallet pressure rather than simply choosing the strongest model throughout.

\paragraph{Comparators.}
We compare PA-DCT with policies a buyer could use without online learning: Random, the three fixed-arm policies Always-P-cheap, Always-P-mid, and Always-P-premium, and BudgetRule, a wallet-threshold heuristic inspired by cost-aware cascade routing~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt}. BudgetRule chooses P-premium above $50\%$ remaining budget, P-mid above $20\%$, and P-cheap below that. We also include a replay True Oracle that peeks at every affordable arm's realized outcome before choosing. It owns its own wallet trajectory, so it is an upper-bound reference rather than an admissible online policy. All non-oracle policies use locked settings across scenarios and seeds.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
The results are read as a trade-off panel rather than a single leaderboard, following cost-aware LLM routing practice~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt}. \emph{Full-horizon mean quality} $\bar q_T=(1/T)\sum_{t<\tau}q_t$ measures service quality and assigns $q_t=0$ after wallet exhaustion, so a policy that goes bankrupt early is penalized directly. \emph{Budget use} $100\sum_{t<\tau}c_t/B$ shows how much of the wallet is spent. \emph{ROI} $=\sum_{t<\tau}q_t/\sum_{t<\tau}c_t$ measures quality delivered per dollar, but it is an efficiency diagnostic rather than a quality objective: a very cheap policy can have high ROI while serving low-quality answers. For the scalar objective, we report per-round \emph{PA-gap} $(R^{\mathrm{TO}}_T-R^\pi_T)/T$ to the replay True Oracle, where $R^\pi_T=\sum_{t<\tau}r_t$; this avoids ranking policies by raw cumulative payment-aware reward when $\lamn$ depends on each policy's own spending path. Finally, \emph{AdaptT} measures shock response with a trailing-$200$ ROI window: recovery to $95\%$ of pre-outage ROI in S2, and capture of the S3 promotion by reaching $110\%$ of pre-promotion ROI. We use AdaptT only for adaptive PA-DCT variants in Table~\ref{tab:ablations}. Claimed differences use paired seeds with bootstrap confidence intervals and paired normal-approximation $p$-values on the corresponding metric.

\paragraph{Learned adaptation.}
The clearest advantage is shock response. Fixed-arm baselines cannot move when the market changes, and BudgetRule only follows a preset wallet threshold rather than observed reliability or price. PA-DCT instead changes allocation from feedback: Fig.~\ref{fig:learning-curves} shows migration away from P-mid during the S2 outage and migration toward P-premium after the S3 price drop. AdaptT in Table~\ref{tab:ablations} quantifies this learned response among adaptive PA-DCT variants: discounting shortens S2 recovery from $2{,}249$ to $1{,}467$ rounds, and the learned cost posterior captures the S3 promotion in the first measurable $200$-round window, whereas the variant that pins costs to the specification never reaches the S3 threshold.

\paragraph{Quality under a budget.}
The same adaptation improves service quality where fixed cheap routing would save money but under-serve tasks. Under S2, PA-DCT reaches $\bar q_T=0.761$ versus $0.610$ for Always-P-cheap and $0.757$ for the outage-exposed Always-P-mid (paired normal-approximation test, $\Delta\bar q_T=+0.004$, $p=1.67\times10^{-3}$), a $+15$ percentage-point gain over the cheapest arm while using $42\%$ of the budget. Under S3, PA-DCT improves over Always-P-mid on every reported axis: $\bar q_T$ ($0.831$ vs $0.819$), ROI ($429$ vs $410$), and PA-gap/$T$ ($0.121$ vs $0.129$). The same-price decoys clarify why feedback matters: P-flaky is kept at low allocation because timeouts provide explicit negative feedback, while P-adv can be harder in open-ended T3b because fluent-but-wrong answers can sometimes pass the judge, an evaluator boundary we return to in \S\ref{sec:discuss}.

\paragraph{Payment-aware trade-off.}
S1 is the stationary sanity check: the fixed reliable mid-tier arm has the lowest non-oracle PA-gap/$T$ ($0.101$), while PA-DCT lands at $0.133$ and trails by $\approx\!2.2$ percentage points in $\bar q_T$ because it keeps exploring and maintaining shock-ready posteriors. This is the price of adaptivity rather than the main deployment target. Conversely, policies that look strong on one column can fail another: Always-P-cheap has the highest ROI but the lowest $\bar q_T$, BudgetRule maximizes $\bar q_T$ in S1/S2 while using $80\%$ of the budget and incurring large PA-gap/$T$, and Always-P-premium maximizes S3 $\bar q_T$ only after the price shock makes that tier cheap enough.

\paragraph{Robustness across axes.}
Across the nine rank-comparable cells (three metrics $\{\bar q_T, \mathrm{ROI}, \mathrm{PA\text{-}gap}/T\}$ across $\{\text{S1},\text{S2},\text{S3}\}$; budget use has no direction-of-preference because lower use correlates with lower quality), PA-DCT is the unique non-oracle policy whose worst rank is $3$ out of $6$. Equivalently, PA-DCT is top-three in all nine rank-comparable cells and top-two in five; the worst ranks are PA-DCT $3/6$, Always-P-mid $4/6$, Random $5/6$, and Always-P-cheap, Always-P-premium, and BudgetRule $6/6$. This is the main table's robustness message: the fixed and rule-based policies can be attractive on one axis, but each has a regime where it ranks poorly on an axis an autonomous agent may care about.

\paragraph{Statistical checks.}
The paired-seed tests support the claimed directions. Across the fifteen PA-DCT-vs-baseline tests on $\bar q_T$, all reach $p<0.002$. On PA-gap/$T$, fourteen reach $p<10^{-3}$ and the remaining S2 comparison against Always-P-cheap is a statistical tie ($0.166$ vs $0.165$, $p=0.17$), marked by $\dagger$ in Table~\ref{tab:main}.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:eval-ablations}

The ablation tests whether PA-DCT's full design is a balanced adaptive policy rather than a per-scenario tuning of one scalar. It is therefore a failure-mode check: we remove each conceptual ingredient---Payment-aware ranking ($-P$), Discount ($-D$), Contextual bucketing ($-C$), and Thompson sampling ($-TS$)---to test which behavior disappears. We also run a price-shock diagnostic, $-C_{\mathrm{post}}$, which keeps wallet pressure but pins cost to the specification scalar $\bar c_a$; this isolates whether a learned realized-cost posterior is necessary in S3. Table~\ref{tab:ablations} reports the ablated variants alongside full PA-DCT.

The table isolates three roles. First, payment-awareness is required for solvency: $-P$ overuses expensive arms, exhausts the budget around round ${\sim}6{,}500$ in S1/S2, and sharply worsens both PA-gap/$T$ and full-horizon quality. Second, shock adaptation depends on discounting and learned realized costs: removing discount slows S2 recovery from $1{,}467$ to $2{,}249$ rounds, while $-C_{\mathrm{post}}$ never reaches the S3 promotion threshold and raises PA-gap/$T$ from $0.121$ to $0.144$. Third, context and Thompson sampling are not monotone improvements on every mean metric, but they prevent brittle behavior: pooling all contexts reacts faster to the uniform S2 outage but slows S3 opportunity capture, while greedy posterior means keep PA-gap/$T$ means close to Full in S1/S2 ($0.130$ vs.\ $0.133$ and $0.175$ vs.\ $0.166$) but show ${\sim}8\times$ larger S1 PA-gap/$T$ seed standard deviation ($0.046$ vs.\ $0.005$) and miss the S3 price shock in $6/30$ seeds. The three roles together cover the failure modes an autonomous buyer faces: budget control, non-stationary response (both reliability and price), and exploration stability under task heterogeneity.
