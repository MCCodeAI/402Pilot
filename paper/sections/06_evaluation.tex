\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:eval-setup}

\paragraph{Benchmark.}
We construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a frozen-replay benchmark with $823$ tasks across coding (HumanEval pass@1), multi-hop QA (HotpotQA max EM/F1), and web search. Web search is split into closed-form T3a (deterministic max EM/F1) and open-ended T3b (cached LLM-as-judge), giving the four evaluation buckets T1/T2/T3a/T3b used by PA-DCT ($K_b=4$). For each (task, provider) pair we pre-generate $V=5$ response versions, yielding $823\times5\times5=20{,}575$ scored responses. The response pool is fixed for reproducibility; non-stationarity enters only through scenario-level transformations of realized failures and effective prices.

\paragraph{Providers.}
We use five heterogeneous provider pipelines, deliberately calibrated to expose the same-tier-different-quality phenomenon central to the regime rather than to model a representative market distribution. Table~\ref{tab:providers} summarizes the configuration; the key design point is that P-mid, P-adv, and P-flaky share both base price (\$0.002/call) and base model, so no static rule can distinguish them---only reward feedback can.

\paragraph{Scenarios and scale.}
We evaluate three calibrated market regimes: S1 stationary operation, S2 a P-mid outage from rounds $3{,}000$--$5{,}500$ with $30\%$ forced failures, and S3 a P-premium promotion at round $1{,}000$ that drops its effective price from \$0.01 to \$0.002. Each (policy, scenario) cell uses $30$ seeds, $T=10{,}000$ rounds, and budget $B=\$50$. At the listed premium price, this budget supports $5{,}000$ premium calls, so S1/S2 expose wallet pressure directly; S3 tests whether a policy can use the lower realized post-shock price rather than continuing to penalize P-premium as a high-cost arm. Within each scenario, policies share the same seed paths and frozen response stream, so paired differences reflect policy behavior under the same replay conditions.

\paragraph{Comparators.}
We compare PA-DCT with five non-oracle baselines: Random, three fixed-arm policies (Always-P-cheap, Always-P-mid, Always-P-premium), and BudgetRule, a fixed wallet-threshold rule inspired by cost-aware cascade heuristics~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt}: it chooses P-premium above $50\%$ remaining budget, P-mid above $20\%$, and P-cheap below that. We also report a replay True Oracle: it peeks at every affordable arm's realized outcome before choosing, owns its own wallet trajectory, and is therefore an upper-bound reference rather than an admissible online policy. All policies use the same locked settings across scenarios and seeds.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
We compute five locked metrics. The primary metric is cumulative payment-aware reward, $R^\pi_T=\sum_t r_t$ (cum-PA); paired-bootstrap significance tests are computed on this quantity. We also report mean quality $\bar q=(1/T)\sum_t q_t$, ROI $=\sum_t q_t/\sum_t c_t$, cumulative regret to the True Oracle, and AdaptT. AdaptT is a trailing-$200$ ROI response time: in S2 it measures recovery to at least $95\%$ of pre-outage ROI, while in S3 it measures capture of the promotion opportunity by reaching at least $110\%$ of pre-promotion ROI. The S3 threshold is fixed before evaluation to require a clear improvement over the pre-shock baseline rather than mere recovery. AdaptT is undefined in S1.

Each scenario shows cum-PA, ROI, and $\bar q$; S2/S3 additionally report AdaptT because shock response is the focal behavior. Table~\ref{tab:main} should be read as an adaptation trade-off rather than a claim that PA-DCT is the best stationary arm. In S1, where no shock occurs, the fixed reliable mid-tier policy achieves the highest non-oracle cum-PA ($5831$), while PA-DCT trails by $\approx\!319$ because it continues to explore and maintain shock-ready posteriors. This gap is the cost of using a policy designed for drifting markets in a market with no drift.

The benefit of that design appears once the market changes. Under the S2 reliability shock, PA-DCT outperforms four of the five non-oracle baselines on cum-PA and statistically ties the remaining cheap-only baseline ($5147$ vs $5164$). The tie is not an equivalence in service quality: Always-P-cheap avoids the outage by never buying the mid-tier arm, but its mean quality remains $0.610$, whereas PA-DCT reaches $0.761$ while also beating Always-P-mid, which is directly exposed to the $30\%$ outage. The middle panel of Fig.~\ref{fig:learning-curves} shows this response as a temporary migration away from P-mid during the outage followed by a return after recovery.

S3 tests a different kind of non-stationarity: a price opportunity rather than a reliability failure. Here the learned cost posterior lets PA-DCT treat the premium discount as feedback instead of static metadata. PA-DCT outperforms all five non-oracle baselines on cum-PA and improves over Always-P-mid on all reported axes: cum-PA ($5911$ vs $5831$), ROI ($429$ vs $410$), and mean quality ($0.831$ vs $0.819$). The right panel of Fig.~\ref{fig:learning-curves} shows the resulting migration to P-premium after the price drop. The same-price decoys also clarify the role of feedback quality: P-flaky is kept at low allocation because timeouts provide explicit negative feedback, while P-adv can be harder in open-ended T3b because fluent-but-wrong answers can sometimes pass the judge, an evaluator boundary we return to in \S\ref{sec:discuss}.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:eval-ablations}

We disable each of PA-DCT's four conceptual components in turn---Payment-aware ranking ($-P$), Discount ($-D$), Contextual bucketing ($-C$), Thompson sampling ($-TS$). Full PA-DCT is a robust shock-adaptive design, not a per-scenario tuning of the highest cum-PA mean, so the ablation asks which component is exposed on which metric. We additionally run a diagnostic ablation that isolates the cost-posterior pathway inside payment-awareness ($-C_{\mathrm{post}}$, which pins cost to the spec scalar $\bar c_a$ while leaving wallet-pressure weighting intact). This diagnostic is meaningful only under price drift, so we run it on S3 only. Table~\ref{tab:ablations} reports the ablated variants alongside full PA-DCT.

Table~\ref{tab:ablations} is intentionally multi-metric: different components fail on different axes. The most direct failure is removing payment-awareness: without wallet pressure, the policy overuses expensive arms and drives cum-PA below zero in S1/S2. Discounting has a different signature. It is not mainly justified by stationary reward, but by response speed, shortening S2 recovery from $2{,}249$ to $1{,}467$ rounds.

Contextual bucketing and Thompson sampling matter in less monotone ways. A single global bucket reacts faster to the uniform S2 outage because evidence is pooled, but contextual buckets help exploit the S3 promotion under task-dependent quality differences ($200$ vs $398$ rounds). Greedy posterior means can be close on average in S1/S2, yet they show $5$--$9\times$ larger seed variance and miss the S3 price shock in $6/30$ seeds, which is precisely the kind of opportunity Thompson sampling is meant to keep discoverable.

The cost-posterior diagnostic isolates the price channel. When costs are pinned to the specification scalar, the policy keeps treating P-premium as expensive after the promotion: P-premium share stays below $4\%$, AdaptT is undefined, and cum-PA drops by $230$ relative to full PA-DCT. Taken together, these ablations show that PA-DCT's components are not exposed by a single leaderboard metric: cum-PA exposes wallet pressure, AdaptT exposes shock response, variance exposes exploration stability, and mean quality exposes whether price adaptation translates into better service quality.
