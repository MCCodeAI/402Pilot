\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

Agent-native payment decision-making is a new evaluation setting: an agent repeatedly chooses among paid providers, spends from a finite wallet, and learns only from post-payment outcomes. Existing LLM benchmarks do not capture this full loop, so we construct 402Pilot-Bench on top of public task datasets that provide recognizable workloads and established scorers. We instantiate this loop by pre-generating and scoring multiple responses for each task--provider pair, then replaying the cached outcomes under shared seeds, provider menus, wallet budgets, and market scenarios. This frozen-replay design preserves real LLM output variation while avoiding repeated live provider calls, which would add cost and confound policy effects with API drift and sampling noise.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:eval-setup}

\paragraph{Benchmark.}
\emph{402Pilot-Bench} contains $823$ tasks across coding (HumanEval pass@1), multi-hop QA (HotpotQA max EM/F1), and web search. Web search is split into closed-form T3a (deterministic max EM/F1) and open-ended T3b (cached LLM-as-judge), yielding the four task buckets T1/T2/T3a/T3b used by PA-DCT ($K_b=4$). The cache stores $V=5$ response versions for each (task, provider) pair, yielding $823\times5\times5=20{,}575$ scored responses. During evaluation, scenario changes modify realized failures or effective prices, not the underlying task pool.

\paragraph{Providers.}
The five providers are arranged along two axes: a visible price--quality ladder (P-cheap/P-mid/P-premium) and a same-price decoy set (P-mid/P-adv/P-flaky) whose base price and base model match but whose outcomes differ. P-adv produces fluent but wrong answers, while P-flaky returns billed timeouts. A selector that only reads static metadata cannot separate these three arms; it has to learn from paid feedback. Table~\ref{tab:providers} summarizes the five pipelines.

\paragraph{Scenarios and scale.}
The same benchmark is replayed under three market regimes. S1 is stationary operation. S2 injects a reliability shock: P-mid has $30\%$ forced failures from rounds $3{,}000$--$5{,}500$ and then recovers. S3 injects a price shock: at round $1{,}000$, P-premium's effective price drops from \$0.01 to \$0.002, matching the mid-tier price. Each (policy, scenario) cell uses $30$ paired seeds, $T=10{,}000$ rounds, and budget $B=\$50$; a seed fixes the task order, response-version draws, and scenario events for all policies in that scenario. At the listed premium price, the budget buys only $5{,}000$ premium calls, so a policy must manage wallet pressure rather than simply choosing the strongest model throughout.

\paragraph{Comparators.}
We compare PA-DCT against three families. First, non-learning buyer policies: Random, three fixed-arm policies, and BudgetRule, a Frugal-style static cascade heuristic under our admissible contract~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt}. BudgetRule chooses P-premium above $50\%$ remaining budget, P-mid above $20\%$, and P-cheap below that. Second, two learning baselines that each cover part of the ingredient set identified in \S\ref{sec:related-work} (Table~\ref{tab:bandit-components}) but not its combination. \emph{Contextual DS-TS} adapts the discounted Thompson sampling rule of \citet{qi2023dsts} to the same task buckets used in \S\ref{sec:method-posteriors}: it maintains a discounted Q-posterior per (arm, bucket) but does not read wallet pressure and does not learn realized cost. \emph{Contextual BTS} adapts the budgeted Thompson sampling rule of \citet{xia2015bts} to Gaussian Q and C posteriors with the same task buckets: it learns a cost posterior alongside utility but applies no discount, and selects by the raw $\hat r / \hat c$ ratio that BTS uses in its published form. The Gaussian C-posterior can produce negative samples in early rounds (a known artifact of replacing Xia's Beta-Bernoulli model with continuous Gaussians); we floor the denominator at $\varepsilon=10^{-6}$ USD as a numerical guard. The asymptotic ratio-scoring behaviour, which is what matters for the comparison, is set by realized cost spread across providers and is unaffected by the floor. Both Bayesian baselines share PA-DCT's priors and likelihood variances and respect the affordable-set mask, so the only differences from PA-DCT are the components under test (wallet-pressure scaling, discount, cost-posterior learning). Third, a replay True Oracle that peeks at every affordable arm's realized outcome before choosing. The oracle owns its own wallet trajectory, so it is an upper-bound reference rather than an admissible online policy. All non-oracle policies use locked settings across scenarios and seeds.

We do not include FrugalGPT-style cascade routing, RouteLLM, or Hybrid LLM as head-to-head baselines because their decision contracts conflict with our post-payment feedback regime: cascade routing assumes published per-call prices and supports sequential probing within a single query; supervised trained routers require offline preference or quality labels that are not produced by our quote--pay--observe protocol; and bandit routers in this line still treat prices as observable rate-card inputs rather than receipt-revealed signals. BudgetRule approximates this family under the \S\ref{sec:problem} contract. Direct adaptation of trained routers would require either re-training under wallet pressure or assuming offline price visibility, neither of which is admissible under our formulation.

\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:eval-main}

PA-DCT is not designed for static optimality in one fixed market; it is a wallet-constrained policy for learning and adapting in complex, changing markets. Against the comparator set defined above, Table~\ref{tab:main} shows that the main result is not one-column dominance but stable adaptation across changing markets. In stationary S1, PA-DCT's exploration creates a small cost relative to the best fixed mid-tier arm. In S2, under the P-mid outage, PA-DCT maintains quality with much less budget than BudgetRule and avoids Always-P-cheap's quality collapse. In S3, it captures the premium promotion, improving over Always-P-mid in quality and ROI while achieving the best non-oracle PA-gap/$T$. The learning baselines sharpen the failure modes: Contextual DS-TS tracks quality drift but lacks wallet pressure and exhausts the budget under S1/S2; Contextual BTS learns stationary cost but optimizes a raw $\hat r / \hat c$ ratio, so under our $20\times$ cost spread it locks onto P-cheap and never explores premium enough to register the S3 price drop. This balance is the point: PA-DCT is not always a one-column winner, but it remains solvent and competitive across quality, ROI, and PA-gap/$T$ without scenario-specific rules.

\paragraph{Learned adaptation.}
The clearest evidence is the allocation curve. Fixed-arm baselines cannot react after deployment, and BudgetRule only follows a preset wallet threshold rather than observed reliability or price. PA-DCT changes allocation from feedback: in Fig.~\ref{fig:learning-curves}, it quickly moves away from P-mid during the S2 outage and shifts toward P-premium after the S3 price drop. In S1, where no shock occurs, the allocation remains mid-dominated, so the same mechanism does not force churn when feedback is stable. The policy moves when the market changes and settles when it does not.

\paragraph{Robustness across axes.}
Taken as a trade-off panel, Table~\ref{tab:main} shows that PA-DCT is the only non-oracle policy without a clear failure mode across the three markets. As a compact robustness descriptor, PA-DCT has the lowest mean rank ($2.89$ on an eight-policy scale) and the smallest worst rank ($4/8$) across the nine rank-comparable cells in Table~\ref{tab:main}. The per-axis numbers in Table~\ref{tab:main} remain the load-bearing evidence, but this summary matches the central finding: the decision layer matters because the buyer must keep learning and adapting under market change, not merely win one static metric. Cheap policies and Contextual BTS conserve budget but under-serve tasks; premium-heavy policies and BudgetRule can buy quality in the wrong regime and pay a large PA-gap; Contextual DS-TS tracks quality drift but lacks wallet pressure and exhausts the budget in S1/S2. PA-DCT does not win every column, but it is the policy that keeps quality, spending efficiency, and payment-aware objective jointly competitive without scenario-specific rules.

\paragraph{Quality under a budget.}
The learned reallocations matter because the cheap safe policy under-serves tasks, while static high-quality policies can spend in the wrong places. In S2, PA-DCT reaches $\bar q_T=0.761$ while spending $43\%$ of the budget, compared with $0.610$ for Always-P-cheap at $10\%$ budget use. Its gain over Always-P-mid is small in absolute quality ($0.761$ vs.\ $0.757$) but significant under paired seeds ($\Delta\bar q_T=+0.004$, $p=1.67\times10^{-3}$), and it comes from learning around the outage rather than following a fixed provider. In S3, PA-DCT improves over Always-P-mid on all three rank-comparable metrics: $\bar q_T$ ($0.831$ vs.\ $0.819$), ROI ($429$ vs.\ $410$), and PA-gap/$T$ ($0.121$ vs.\ $0.129$). Always-P-premium reaches the highest S3 quality once premium becomes cheap, but it does so with lower ROI and much worse PA-gap because it pays for premium before the promotion as well.

\paragraph{Payment-aware trade-off.}
S1 is the stationary sanity check and the place where PA-DCT's cost is visible. The fixed reliable mid-tier arm has the lowest non-oracle PA-gap/$T$ ($0.101$), while PA-DCT lands at $0.133$ and trails by about $2.2$ percentage points in $\bar q_T$ because it keeps exploring and maintaining shock-ready posteriors. We read this as the price of adaptivity rather than a failure in the stationary market. The main table also shows why no single static baseline is enough: Always-P-cheap has the highest ROI but the lowest $\bar q_T$, BudgetRule maximizes $\bar q_T$ in S1/S2 while using $80\%$ of the budget and incurring large PA-gap/$T$, and Always-P-premium only becomes attractive after S3 makes its target arm cheap.

\paragraph{Statistical checks.}
Paired-seed tests match these claims. The positive S2/S3 comparisons above are significant, including S2 quality versus Always-P-mid ($p=1.67\times10^{-3}$) and S3 improvements over Always-P-mid on quality, ROI, and PA-gap/$T$ (all $p<10^{-20}$). PA-DCT also has a significantly smaller PA-gap/$T$ than both learning baselines in S1 and S3 at $p<10^{-100}$ in every cell. The S1 disadvantages against Always-P-mid and BudgetRule are similarly significant, which is why we treat them as an exploration cost rather than hide them. The one cell where PA-DCT's PA-gap/$T$ is not statistically distinguishable from a baseline is S2 against Always-P-cheap ($0.166$ vs.\ $0.164$, $p=0.17$), marked by $\dagger$ in Table~\ref{tab:main}; the same paired test also returns $p=0.86$ for PA-DCT versus Contextual BTS on this cell, while a separate paired test between Always-P-cheap and Contextual BTS rejects equivalence ($p=0.03$). The S2 lower bound of PA-gap/$T$ is therefore a three-way overlap that is not transitive, not a single equivalence class.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:eval-ablations}

The ablation tests whether PA-DCT's full design is a balanced adaptive policy rather than a per-scenario tuning of one scalar. It is therefore a failure-mode check: we remove each conceptual ingredient---Payment-aware ranking ($-P$), Discount ($-D$), Contextual bucketing ($-C$), and Thompson sampling ($-TS$)---to test which behavior disappears. We also run a price-shock diagnostic, $-C_{\mathrm{post}}$, which keeps wallet pressure but pins cost to the specification scalar $\bar c_a$; this isolates whether a learned realized-cost posterior is necessary in S3. Ablations isolate the role of each PA-DCT component while sharing the same implementation infrastructure; head-to-head comparisons against adapted published learning baselines (Contextual DS-TS, Contextual BTS) are reported in \S\ref{sec:eval-main}. Table~\ref{tab:ablations} reports the ablated variants alongside full PA-DCT.

The table isolates three roles. First, payment-awareness is required for solvency: $-P$ overuses expensive arms, exhausts the budget around round ${\sim}6{,}500$ in S1/S2, and sharply worsens both PA-gap/$T$ and full-horizon quality. Second, shock adaptation depends on discounting and learned realized costs: removing discount slows S2 recovery from $1{,}467$ to $2{,}249$ rounds, while $-C_{\mathrm{post}}$ never reaches the S3 promotion threshold and raises PA-gap/$T$ from $0.121$ to $0.144$. Third, context and Thompson sampling are not monotone improvements on every mean metric, but they prevent brittle behavior: pooling all contexts reacts faster to the uniform S2 outage but slows S3 opportunity capture, while greedy posterior means keep PA-gap/$T$ means close to Full in S1/S2 ($0.130$ vs.\ $0.133$ and $0.175$ vs.\ $0.166$) but show ${\sim}8\times$ larger S1 PA-gap/$T$ seed standard deviation ($0.046$ vs.\ $0.005$) and miss the S3 price shock in $6/30$ seeds. The three roles together cover the failure modes an autonomous buyer faces: budget control, non-stationary response (both reliability and price), and exploration stability under task heterogeneity.
