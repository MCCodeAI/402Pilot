\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{402Pilot-Bench setup}
\label{sec:eval-setup}

\paragraph{Pre-generated dataset.}
We construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a replay-based benchmark in which all LLM responses are pre-generated and frozen. The dataset comprises $824$ tasks across coding (HumanEval; pass@1), multi-hop QA (HotpotQA; max EM/F1 against gold), and web search (TriviaQA closed-form, plus a custom open-ended subset; LLM-as-judge with a structured rubric). For each (task, provider) pair we pre-generate $V = 5$ response versions, yielding $20{,}575$ total responses scored once at pre-generation time and replayed deterministically across all bandit experiments. Pre-generation decouples bandit evaluation from inference noise and enables exact reproduction of any seed; it also caps the cost of large-scale ablation sweeps by amortizing inference once across thousands of replays.

\paragraph{Task types.}
Tasks fall into four types treated separately because their evaluators differ: T1 coding (HumanEval pass@1, deterministic), T2 multi-hop QA (HotpotQA max EM/F1, deterministic given gold), T3a web-search closed-form (TriviaQA-web, deterministic max EM/F1), T3b web-search open-ended (custom, LLM-as-judge against a fixed rubric, cached at pre-generation). PA-DCT's contextual bucketing operates at the granularity of these four types ($K_b = 4$).

\paragraph{Scale.}
Each (policy, scenario) cell runs $N = 30$ seeds for $T = 10{,}000$ rounds with budget $B = \$50$, sized so that Always-P-premium exhausts the wallet near round $5{,}000$, ensuring that budget pressure is materially felt by every adaptive policy in the second half of every run. All policies and scenarios are evaluated on identical seed paths and the same frozen response set, so between-cell variance is attributable only to the policy.

\subsection{Comparators and metrics}
\label{sec:eval-comparators}

\paragraph{Comparators.}
Six baselines plus an offline upper bound, all evaluated on identical seeds:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Random.} Uniform draw from the affordable set each round.
\item \textbf{Always-P-cheap / Always-P-mid / Always-P-premium.} Three fixed-arm policies covering the price tiers; Always-P-premium is the strongest non-adaptive baseline on quality alone.

\item \textbf{PA-DCT.} Ours.
\item \textbf{True Oracle (upper bound).} A free-running policy that, each round, peeks at every affordable arm's actual outcome at the round's realized response version and picks the one with the highest payment-aware reward at the wallet's current $\lambda_t$. The Oracle owns its own wallet and so its own $\lambda$ trajectory; it is the upper bound for the scenario, not for any fixed-$\lambda$ trajectory.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Metrics.}
Four metrics, locked in advance:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Task success rate.} Mean per-round quality $\bar q = (1/T)\sum_t q_t$, a proxy for task-level success at the granularity of the per-task evaluator.
\item \emph{ROI.} $\sum_t q_t / \sum_t c_t$, the quality yield per dollar.
\item \emph{Cumulative regret.} $R^{\mathrm{Oracle}}_T - R^{\pi}_T$, the gap to True Oracle on cumulative payment-aware reward.
\item \emph{Adaptation time.} Rounds for the trailing-$200$-round ROI to recover within $5\%$ of its pre-event level after a shock (S2 and S3 only; undefined in S1).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Headline.}
On 402Pilot-Bench, PA-DCT outperforms every baseline on cumulative payment-aware reward in both non-stationary scenarios (S2 outage and S3 promo), with paired-bootstrap significance at $p<10^{-3}$ in $13$ of the $14$ non-stationary baseline cells (the only \texttt{ns} cell is S2 vs Always-P-cheap, where the cheap arm escapes the outage by accident; see narrative below). In the stationary scenario S1, PA-DCT trails Always-P-mid by a small exploration cost ($\Delta_{\mathrm{cum\text{-}PA}} \approx -319$, $p<10^{-3}$ in PA-DCT's disfavour); the same regime where this cost is paid (no drift) is the one where adaptation provides no value.

\paragraph{Per-baseline narrative.}
\textbf{vs Random and Always-P-cheap.} PA-DCT dominates on quality and ROI across every scenario. Random spreads the wallet but samples adversarial and flaky arms equally with reliable ones, dragging mean quality. Always-P-cheap stays solvent but produces low-quality outputs; it is the only baseline that ties PA-DCT on cum-PA in S2 (because Always-P-cheap is not affected by P-mid's outage), but loses on every other metric and every other scenario.
\textbf{vs Always-P-mid.} The closest competitor on cum-PA in S1 (PA-DCT slightly trails), but PA-DCT outperforms on ROI by routing easier tasks to P-cheap. In S2, Always-P-mid is heavily penalized by the $30\%$ failure rate during the outage window; PA-DCT detects degradation within $\approx 1{,}500$ rounds and migrates spend.
\textbf{vs Always-P-premium.} Always-P-premium achieves the highest mean quality but exhausts the wallet near round $5{,}000$ in every scenario, accumulating large negative payment-aware reward thereafter; PA-DCT achieves comparable mean quality at a fraction of the spend.
\textbf{vs BudgetRule.} BudgetRule's static escalation triggers at fixed wallet thresholds and cannot detect the S3 price promotion. PA-DCT migrates to P-premium within $\approx 200$ rounds of the round-$1{,}000$ shock and accumulates substantially more cum-PA over the remaining $9{,}000$ rounds.

\paragraph{Adversarial-provider detection.}
A side-effect of contextual updates: PA-DCT identifies P-adv as low-utility within the first $\approx 1{,}000$ rounds on T1/T2 (deterministic evaluators) and avoids it thereafter. On T3b open-ended (LLM-as-judge), P-adv detection is slower and less complete because the evaluator occasionally accepts fluent-but-wrong answers; this is an evaluator limit, not a policy limit, and we revisit it in \S\ref{sec:discuss}.

\paragraph{$-P$ (no payment-aware): uniformly fatal.}
Removing the budget-pressure weighting forces selection by raw utility, causing the policy to favour high-quality but expensive arms and exhaust the wallet early. Cumulative reward collapses to negative across all three scenarios: $\mathrm{cum\text{-}PA} = -1{,}710 \pm 588$ in S1, $-1{,}966 \pm 507$ in S2, and only $+4{,}528 \pm 369$ in S3 because the round-$1{,}000$ price drop bails the policy out post-shock. \emph{Conclusion}: payment-awareness is necessary in the agent-native regime in absolute terms; no other ingredient compensates.

\paragraph{$-C$ (no contextual, $K_b = 1$): scenario-dependent.}
A single global bucket actually \emph{helps} adaptation in S2 ($1{,}176$ vs full $1{,}467$ rounds): the outage hits all task types uniformly, so per-bucket fragmentation slows the policy down. In S3, where the price shock makes P-premium especially valuable on hard tasks (T3b), bucketing pays off: full PA-DCT adapts in $200$ rounds vs $-C$'s $398$. \emph{Conclusion}: contextual bucketing helps when shock structure is heterogeneous across task types; when the shock is uniform, it adds overhead without information. Practitioners should tune $K_b$ to expected drift heterogeneity.

\paragraph{$-TS$ (no Thompson sampling, greedy posterior mean): variance, not mean.}
Greedy variants achieve cum-PA within $\pm 50$ of full PA-DCT on every scenario, but seed-to-seed standard deviation is $5$--$9\times$ higher (full $\approx 50$--$80$; $-TS$ $244$--$481$). More tellingly, in S3 the greedy variant fails to detect the price shock in $6$ of $30$ seeds (adaptation\_time $2{,}232$ vs full $200$, an $11\times$ slowdown when it does detect, undefined in the $6$ that do not). \emph{Conclusion}: Thompson sampling is what makes shock detection routine instead of luck-of-initialization-dependent; the cum-PA-mean similarity hides this.

\paragraph{Why four metrics, not one.}
No single classical bandit metric reveals all four contributions. Cumulative reward alone misses $-D$'s contribution entirely and underweights $-TS$'s variance-control role. ROI alone over-rewards $-TS$ (greedy locks onto cheap arms with high q/\$ ratio while missing better-quality opportunities). Adaptation\_time without cumulative reward would miss $-P$'s catastrophic collapse. The four metrics together expose each component on the axis where it matters; this is the multi-metric evaluation framework we recommend for future work in this regime.
