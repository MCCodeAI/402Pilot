\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

Agent-native payment decision-making is a new evaluation setting: an agent repeatedly chooses among paid providers, spends from a finite wallet, and learns only from post-payment outcomes. Existing LLM benchmarks do not capture this full loop, so we construct 402Pilot-Bench on top of public task datasets that provide recognizable workloads and established scorers. We instantiate this loop by pre-generating and scoring multiple responses for each task--provider pair, then replaying the cached outcomes under shared seeds, provider menus, wallet budgets, and market scenarios. This frozen-replay design preserves real LLM output variation while avoiding repeated live provider calls, which would add cost and confound policy effects with API drift and sampling noise.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:eval-setup}

\paragraph{Benchmark.}
\emph{402Pilot-Bench} contains $823$ tasks across coding (HumanEval pass@1), multi-hop QA (HotpotQA max EM/F1), and web search. Web search is split into closed-form T3a (deterministic max EM/F1) and open-ended T3b (cached LLM-as-judge), yielding the four task buckets T1/T2/T3a/T3b used by PA-DCT ($K_b=4$). The cache stores $V=5$ response versions for each (task, provider) pair, yielding $823\times5\times5=20{,}575$ scored responses. During evaluation, scenario changes modify realized failures or effective prices, not the underlying task pool.

\paragraph{Providers.}
The five providers are arranged along two axes: a visible price--quality ladder (P-cheap/P-mid/P-premium) and a same-price decoy set (P-mid/P-adv/P-flaky) whose base price and base model match but whose outcomes differ. P-adv produces fluent but wrong answers, while P-flaky returns billed timeouts. A selector that only reads static metadata cannot separate these three arms; it has to learn from paid feedback. Table~\ref{tab:providers} summarizes the five pipelines.

\paragraph{Scenarios and scale.}
The same benchmark is replayed under three market regimes. S1 is stationary operation. S2 injects a reliability shock: P-mid has $30\%$ forced failures from rounds $3{,}000$--$5{,}500$ and then recovers. S3 injects a price shock: at round $1{,}000$, P-premium's effective price drops from \$0.01 to \$0.002, matching the mid-tier price. Each (policy, scenario) cell uses $30$ paired seeds, $T=10{,}000$ rounds, and budget $B=\$50$; a seed fixes the task order, response-version draws, and scenario events for all policies in that scenario. At the listed premium price, the budget buys only $5{,}000$ premium calls, so a policy must manage wallet pressure rather than simply choosing the strongest model throughout.

\paragraph{Comparators.}
We compare PA-DCT with policies a buyer could use without online learning: Random, the three fixed-arm policies Always-P-cheap, Always-P-mid, and Always-P-premium, and BudgetRule, a wallet-threshold heuristic inspired by cost-aware cascade routing~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt}. BudgetRule chooses P-premium above $50\%$ remaining budget, P-mid above $20\%$, and P-cheap below that. We also include a replay True Oracle that peeks at every affordable arm's realized outcome before choosing. It owns its own wallet trajectory, so it is an upper-bound reference rather than an admissible online policy. All non-oracle policies use locked settings across scenarios and seeds.

\paragraph{Metrics and statistical protocol.}
The results are read as a trade-off panel rather than a single leaderboard, following cost-aware LLM routing practice~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt}. Table~\ref{tab:metrics} defines the reported metrics. ROI is an efficiency diagnostic rather than a quality objective: a very cheap policy can have high ROI while serving low-quality answers.

\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:eval-main}

PA-DCT's main result is adaptive spending: it learns from paid outcomes and reallocates a finite wallet when provider reliability or price changes. It is not meant to top every metric in the stationary case. Its purpose is to stay solvent, move away from providers whose realized quality drops, and redirect budget toward newly attractive providers without hand-written scenario rules. Table~\ref{tab:main} and Fig.~\ref{fig:learning-curves} show this pattern: PA-DCT pays a modest exploration cost in S1, moves away from P-mid during the S2 outage, and captures the S3 premium promotion while staying strong across the rank-comparable metrics.

\paragraph{Learned adaptation.}
The clearest evidence is the allocation curve. Fixed-arm baselines cannot react after deployment, and BudgetRule only follows a preset wallet threshold rather than observed reliability or price. PA-DCT changes allocation from feedback: in Fig.~\ref{fig:learning-curves}, it leaves P-mid during the S2 outage and shifts toward P-premium after the S3 price drop. In S1, where no shock occurs, the allocation remains mid-dominated, so the same mechanism does not force churn when feedback is stable. This is the behavior the layer is meant to enable: payment execution becomes a signal for learning what should be bought next.

\paragraph{Quality under a budget.}
The learned reallocations matter because the cheap safe policy under-serves tasks, while static high-quality policies can spend in the wrong places. In S2, PA-DCT reaches $\bar q_T=0.761$ while spending $42\%$ of the budget, compared with $0.610$ for Always-P-cheap at $10\%$ budget use. Its gain over Always-P-mid is small in absolute quality ($0.761$ vs.\ $0.757$) but significant under paired seeds ($\Delta\bar q_T=+0.004$, $p=1.67\times10^{-3}$), and it comes from learning around the outage rather than following a fixed provider. In S3, PA-DCT improves over Always-P-mid on all three rank-comparable metrics: $\bar q_T$ ($0.831$ vs.\ $0.819$), ROI ($429$ vs.\ $410$), and PA-gap/$T$ ($0.121$ vs.\ $0.129$). Always-P-premium reaches the highest S3 quality once premium becomes cheap, but it does so with lower ROI and much worse PA-gap because it pays for premium before the promotion as well.

\paragraph{Payment-aware trade-off.}
S1 is the stationary sanity check and the place where PA-DCT's cost is visible. The fixed reliable mid-tier arm has the lowest non-oracle PA-gap/$T$ ($0.101$), while PA-DCT lands at $0.133$ and trails by about $2.2$ percentage points in $\bar q_T$ because it keeps exploring and maintaining shock-ready posteriors. We read this as the price of adaptivity, not as a stationary-market win. The main table also shows why no single static baseline is enough: Always-P-cheap has the highest ROI but the lowest $\bar q_T$, BudgetRule maximizes $\bar q_T$ in S1/S2 while using $80\%$ of the budget and incurring large PA-gap/$T$, and Always-P-premium only becomes attractive after S3 makes its target arm cheap.

\paragraph{Robustness across axes.}
This is the main table's robustness message. Across quality, ROI, and PA-gap/$T$ in S1/S2/S3, PA-DCT is the only non-oracle policy that never falls below third place. The exact ranking is less important than the failure pattern: every fixed or rule-based policy has a regime where it ranks poorly on quality, efficiency, or payment-aware objective, while PA-DCT remains consistently near the top without scenario-specific rules.

\paragraph{Statistical checks.}
Paired-seed tests match these claims. The positive S2/S3 comparisons above are significant, including S2 quality versus Always-P-mid ($p=1.67\times10^{-3}$) and S3 improvements over Always-P-mid on quality, ROI, and PA-gap/$T$ (all $p<10^{-20}$). The S1 disadvantages against Always-P-mid and BudgetRule are also significant, which is why we treat them as an exploration cost rather than hide them. On PA-gap/$T$, the only non-significant PA-DCT comparison is the S2 tie with Always-P-cheap ($0.166$ vs.\ $0.165$, $p=0.17$), marked by $\dagger$ in Table~\ref{tab:main}.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:eval-ablations}

The ablation tests whether PA-DCT's full design is a balanced adaptive policy rather than a per-scenario tuning of one scalar. It is therefore a failure-mode check: we remove each conceptual ingredient---Payment-aware ranking ($-P$), Discount ($-D$), Contextual bucketing ($-C$), and Thompson sampling ($-TS$)---to test which behavior disappears. We also run a price-shock diagnostic, $-C_{\mathrm{post}}$, which keeps wallet pressure but pins cost to the specification scalar $\bar c_a$; this isolates whether a learned realized-cost posterior is necessary in S3. Table~\ref{tab:ablations} reports the ablated variants alongside full PA-DCT.

The table isolates three roles. First, payment-awareness is required for solvency: $-P$ overuses expensive arms, exhausts the budget around round ${\sim}6{,}500$ in S1/S2, and sharply worsens both PA-gap/$T$ and full-horizon quality. Second, shock adaptation depends on discounting and learned realized costs: removing discount slows S2 recovery from $1{,}467$ to $2{,}249$ rounds, while $-C_{\mathrm{post}}$ never reaches the S3 promotion threshold and raises PA-gap/$T$ from $0.121$ to $0.144$. Third, context and Thompson sampling are not monotone improvements on every mean metric, but they prevent brittle behavior: pooling all contexts reacts faster to the uniform S2 outage but slows S3 opportunity capture, while greedy posterior means keep PA-gap/$T$ means close to Full in S1/S2 ($0.130$ vs.\ $0.133$ and $0.175$ vs.\ $0.166$) but show ${\sim}8\times$ larger S1 PA-gap/$T$ seed standard deviation ($0.046$ vs.\ $0.005$) and miss the S3 price shock in $6/30$ seeds. The three roles together cover the failure modes an autonomous buyer faces: budget control, non-stationary response (both reliability and price), and exploration stability under task heterogeneity.
