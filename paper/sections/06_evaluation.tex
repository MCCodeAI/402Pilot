\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:eval-setup}

\paragraph{Benchmark.}
We construct \emph{402Pilot-Bench}, a frozen-replay benchmark with $823$ tasks across coding (HumanEval pass@1), multi-hop QA (HotpotQA max EM/F1), and web search. Web search is split into closed-form T3a (deterministic max EM/F1) and open-ended T3b (cached LLM-as-judge), giving the four evaluation buckets T1/T2/T3a/T3b used by PA-DCT ($K_b=4$). For each (task, provider) pair we pre-generate $V=5$ response versions, yielding $823\times5\times5=20{,}575$ scored responses. The response pool is fixed for reproducibility; non-stationarity enters only through scenario-level transformations of realized failures and effective prices.

\paragraph{Providers.}
We use five heterogeneous provider pipelines, deliberately calibrated to expose the same-tier-different-quality phenomenon central to the regime rather than to model a representative market distribution. Table~\ref{tab:providers} summarizes the configuration; the key design point is that P-mid, P-adv, and P-flaky share both base price (\$0.002/call) and base model, so no static rule can distinguish them---only reward feedback can.

\paragraph{Scenarios and scale.}
We evaluate three calibrated market regimes: S1 stationary operation, S2 a P-mid outage from rounds $3{,}000$--$5{,}500$ with $30\%$ forced failures, and S3 a P-premium promotion at round $1{,}000$ that drops its effective price from \$0.01 to \$0.002. Each (policy, scenario) cell uses $30$ seeds, $T=10{,}000$ rounds, and budget $B=\$50$. At the listed premium price, this budget supports $5{,}000$ premium calls, so S1/S2 expose wallet pressure directly; S3 tests whether a policy can use the lower realized post-shock price rather than continuing to penalize P-premium as a high-cost arm. Within each scenario, policies share the same seed paths and frozen response stream, so paired differences reflect policy behavior under the same replay conditions.

\paragraph{Comparators.}
We compare PA-DCT with five non-oracle baselines: Random, three fixed-arm policies (Always-P-cheap, Always-P-mid, Always-P-premium), and BudgetRule, a fixed wallet-threshold rule inspired by cost-aware cascade heuristics~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt}: it chooses P-premium above $50\%$ remaining budget, P-mid above $20\%$, and P-cheap below that. We also report a replay True Oracle: it peeks at every affordable arm's realized outcome before choosing, owns its own wallet trajectory, and is therefore an upper-bound reference rather than an admissible online policy. All policies use the same locked settings across scenarios and seeds.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
We report five locked metrics as a multi-metric leaderboard rather than a single primary score, following the quality--cost trade-off convention of cost-aware LLM routing benchmarks~\citep{chen2023frugalgpt}. \emph{Full-horizon mean quality} $\bar q_T = (1/T)\sum_{t<\tau} q_t$ measures service quality; unserved rounds after wallet exhaustion ($t \geq \tau$) contribute $q_t=0$, so $\bar q_T$ correctly penalizes premature bankruptcy. \emph{Total spend} $\sum_{t<\tau} c_t$ verifies budget feasibility and \emph{ROI} $=\sum_{t<\tau} q_t/\sum_{t<\tau} c_t$ measures economic efficiency. ROI is therefore an efficiency diagnostic rather than a service-quality objective: very low-cost policies can score high ROI by under-spending, so ROI must be interpreted jointly with $\bar q_T$ and spend. PA-DCT optimizes cumulative payment-aware reward $R^\pi_T=\sum_{t<\tau} r_t$, but because its wallet-pressure weight $\lamn$ depends on each policy's own spending trajectory, we report the induced \emph{PA-gap} $R^{\mathrm{TO}}_T-R^\pi_T$ to the replay True Oracle rather than using raw cum-PA as a stand-alone leaderboard column. \emph{AdaptT} is a trailing-$200$ ROI response time: in S2 it measures recovery to at least $95\%$ of pre-outage ROI, while in S3 it measures capture of the promotion opportunity by reaching at least $110\%$ of pre-promotion ROI. The S3 threshold is fixed before evaluation. AdaptT is undefined in S1; for policies that bankrupt before the shock window completes, AdaptT is reported as \texttt{runout}. Paired-bootstrap significance tests are computed on each claimed metric separately: quality claims on $\bar q_T$, scalar-objective claims on PA-gap, efficiency claims on ROI.

\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:eval-main}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports the locked metrics across S1/S2/S3, as means over $30$ seeds. Each scenario shows $\bar q_T$, total spend, ROI, and PA-gap; the comparisons should be read across axes rather than as a single-column ranking. Across the fifteen pairwise PA-DCT vs.\ baseline tests on $\bar q_T$ (paired bootstrap, two-sided), fourteen reach $p<10^{-3}$ and one reaches $p=1.67\times 10^{-3}$ (S2, vs.\ Always-P-mid); on PA-gap fourteen reach $p<10^{-3}$ and one is a non-significant tie ($p=0.17$, S2 vs.\ Always-P-cheap). Note that significant differences include comparisons where PA-DCT loses on $\bar q_T$ (BudgetRule in S1/S2, Always-P-premium and BudgetRule in S3); the significance tests verify the direction of each cell, not that PA-DCT dominates every axis. Different non-oracle policies top different axes: Always-P-cheap on ROI and S2 PA-gap, Always-P-mid on S1 PA-gap, Always-P-premium on S3 $\bar q_T$ after the price shock, BudgetRule on S1/S2 $\bar q_T$, and PA-DCT on S3 PA-gap. S1 exposes the cost of maintaining adaptivity, while S2/S3 show its value under reliability and price shocks.

Table~\ref{tab:main} should be read as an adaptation trade-off across multiple axes rather than a claim that PA-DCT dominates a single metric in every scenario. No single non-oracle policy is best on every column: BudgetRule maximizes $\bar q_T$ in S1/S2 at the cost of a large PA-gap, Always-P-cheap maximizes ROI uniformly at the cost of the lowest $\bar q_T$, Always-P-mid minimizes PA-gap in S1, and Always-P-premium maximizes $\bar q_T$ in S3 once the price shock makes its tier affordable.

In S1, where no shock occurs, the fixed reliable mid-tier policy achieves the lowest non-oracle PA-gap ($1006$) at $\bar q_T = 0.819$, while PA-DCT lands at PA-gap $1325$ and $\bar q_T = 0.797$, a $\approx\!2.2$ percentage-point quality gap reflecting continued exploration and shock-ready posteriors. The fixed wallet-threshold BudgetRule delivers higher $\bar q_T = 0.831$ but burns \$40 of the \$50 budget to do so, driving PA-gap to $6919$; this is the prototype of a policy that wins on a single axis but is penalized by the scalar objective. PA-DCT's $\$21$ spend tracks Always-P-mid's $\$20$ while keeping the posteriors live for the shocks tested in S2/S3.

The value of that adaptivity appears once the market changes. Under the S2 reliability shock, PA-DCT delivers $\bar q_T = 0.761$ versus $0.610$ for Always-P-cheap and $0.757$ for Always-P-mid---the latter directly exposed to the $30\%$ outage---and the quality advantage over Always-P-cheap is highly significant (paired bootstrap, $\Delta\bar q_T = +0.151$, $p<10^{-4}$). On PA-gap, PA-DCT statistically ties Always-P-cheap ($1662$ vs $1645$, $p=0.17$); this tie reflects the cheap policy's very low wallet pressure ($\lamn$ stays small because Always-P-cheap spends only \$5 of the \$50 budget, so the wallet-pressure penalty on its scalar reward is negligible) rather than comparable service quality, which differs by $15$ percentage points of $\bar q_T$. The middle panel of Fig.~\ref{fig:learning-curves} shows the response as a temporary migration away from P-mid during the outage followed by a return after recovery. PA-DCT's shock-response time (trailing-$200$ ROI recovers to $95\%$ of pre-outage in $1{,}467$ rounds) is reported in Table~\ref{tab:ablations}, where it is compared against PA-DCT variants that share its adaptive structure; fixed-arm baselines have no analogous quantity because they do not adapt.

S3 tests a different kind of non-stationarity: a price opportunity rather than a reliability failure. Here the learned cost posterior lets PA-DCT treat the premium discount as feedback instead of static metadata. PA-DCT has the lowest non-oracle PA-gap and improves over Always-P-mid on every reported axis: PA-gap ($1206$ vs $1286$), ROI ($429$ vs $410$), and $\bar q_T$ ($0.831$ vs $0.819$). The right panel of Fig.~\ref{fig:learning-curves} shows the resulting migration to P-premium after the price drop. The same-price decoys also clarify the role of feedback quality: P-flaky is kept at low allocation because timeouts provide explicit negative feedback, while P-adv can be harder in open-ended T3b because fluent-but-wrong answers can sometimes pass the judge, an evaluator boundary we return to in \S\ref{sec:discuss}.

Read together, the four columns of Table~\ref{tab:main} expose a deployment-relevant robustness property that no single column captures. Across the nine rank-comparable cells (three metrics $\{\bar q_T, \mathrm{ROI}, \mathrm{PA\text{-}gap}\}$ across $\{\text{S1},\text{S2},\text{S3}\}$; spend has no direction-of-preference because lower spend correlates with lower quality), PA-DCT is the unique non-oracle policy whose worst rank is $3$ out of $6$. Equivalently, PA-DCT is top-three in all nine rank-comparable cells and top-two in five; the worst ranks are PA-DCT $3/6$, Always-P-mid $4/6$, Random $5/6$, and Always-P-cheap, Always-P-premium, and BudgetRule $6/6$. The last-rank cells also differ by failure mode: Always-P-cheap is last on $\bar q_T$ in S3, Always-P-premium is last throughout S1/S2, and BudgetRule is last on ROI and PA-gap in S3. This suggests that PA-DCT is the least brittle non-oracle policy in this calibrated comparator set: the fixed and rule-based policies can be attractive on one axis, but each has a regime where it ranks poorly on an axis an autonomous agent may care about.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:eval-ablations}

We disable each of PA-DCT's four conceptual components in turn---Payment-aware ranking ($-P$), Discount ($-D$), Contextual bucketing ($-C$), Thompson sampling ($-TS$). Full PA-DCT is a robust shock-adaptive design, not a per-scenario tuning of the smallest PA-gap, so the ablation asks which component is exposed on which metric. We additionally run a diagnostic ablation that isolates the cost-posterior pathway inside payment-awareness ($-C_{\mathrm{post}}$, which pins cost to the spec scalar $\bar c_a$ while leaving wallet-pressure weighting intact). This diagnostic is meaningful only under price drift, so we run it on S3 only. Table~\ref{tab:ablations} reports the ablated variants alongside full PA-DCT.

Table~\ref{tab:ablations} is intentionally multi-metric: different components fail on different axes. The most direct failure is removing payment-awareness: without wallet pressure the policy overuses expensive arms, exhausts the budget at round ${\sim}6{,}500$ in S1/S2, and drives both PA-gap and $\bar q_T$ far from the cheap-only baseline because of unserved rounds. Discounting has a different signature: it is not mainly justified by stationary reward, but by response speed, shortening S2 recovery from $2{,}249$ to $1{,}467$ rounds.

Contextual bucketing and Thompson sampling matter in less monotone ways. A single global bucket reacts faster to the uniform S2 outage because evidence is pooled, but contextual buckets help exploit the S3 promotion under task-dependent quality differences ($200$ vs $398$ rounds). Greedy posterior means can be close on average in S1/S2, yet they show $5$--$9\times$ larger seed variance and miss the S3 price shock in $6/30$ seeds, which is precisely the kind of opportunity Thompson sampling is meant to keep discoverable.

The cost-posterior diagnostic isolates the price channel. When costs are pinned to the specification scalar, the policy keeps treating P-premium as expensive after the promotion: P-premium share stays below $4\%$, AdaptT is undefined, and PA-gap increases by $230$ relative to full PA-DCT. Taken together, these ablations show that PA-DCT's components are not exposed by a single leaderboard metric: PA-gap exposes wallet pressure, AdaptT exposes shock response, variance exposes exploration stability, and full-horizon quality exposes whether price adaptation translates into better service quality.
