\section{The 402Pilot Decision Layer}
\label{sec:layer}

402Pilot is a decision layer between an autonomous agent and a programmable payment protocol such as x402. The layer accepts a task and a wallet state, picks a provider, calls the protocol stack to settle the payment, and feeds the realized outcome back into a policy. It does not modify the protocol, never holds funds, and never crosses the settlement boundary. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} sketches the per-round flow.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/402pilotlayer.png}
\caption{The 402Pilot decision-layer architecture. The layer encodes the task context, restricts selection to affordable providers, and calls \texttt{policy.select}; settlement and service execution remain outside the layer. After the provider responds, the outcome scorer converts the raw response into $(q_t,c_t,f_t)$, and \texttt{policy.update} receives utility $u_t=q_t-\nu f_t$ together with observed cost $c_t$, not the payment-aware reward $r_t$.}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

The architectural contract is intentionally small. A policy is any object exposing
\begin{align*}
&\mathtt{select}(\mathbf{x}_t,\, \mathcal{A}_t) \to a_t \in \mathcal{A}_t, \\
&\mathtt{update}(\mathbf{x}_t,\, a_t,\, \ut,\, c_t) \to \emptyset,
\end{align*}
where $\mathcal{A}_t \subseteq \{1, \ldots, K\}$ is the set of providers that fit in the remaining wallet at the current effective prices. Affordability is enforced by the wallet, not by the policy: a policy only chooses from a feasible set. The two methods are therefore the only points of contact with the rest of the stack; priors, posteriors, and other internal state remain private to the policy. Wallet pressure is not part of the method signature. A payment-aware policy may hold a reference to the wallet object and read $\lambda_t$ inside \texttt{select}, while non-payment-aware comparators such as Random and Always-X carry no wallet dependency. This keeps the interface uniform across all comparators used in \S\ref{sec:eval-comparators}.

A key design choice is that the layer feeds \emph{utility} $\ut$ to \texttt{policy.update}, rather than the payment-aware reward $\rt$. The reward $\rt$ includes the current wallet-pressure term and is used for selection and evaluation, but it should not be treated as an observation of provider quality. Updating posteriors on $\rt$ would make the same provider appear worse late in a budget-tight run than early in the run, even if its service behavior had not changed. In 402Pilot, beliefs about provider utility and cost are updated from $(\ut,c_t)$, while decision-time budget pressure enters only through \texttt{select}.

The same contract gives the layer two forms of pluggability. On the policy side, every comparator we evaluate---Random, the three Always-X variants, BudgetRule, and PA-DCT itself---implements the same two methods, so algorithms can be exchanged without changing the surrounding runtime. On the protocol side, 402Pilot is agnostic to the programmable payment substrate below it. We instantiate the layer above x402~\citep{coinbase2025x402v2}, but the interface also applies to AP2~\citep{google2025ap2}, A2A--x402~\citep{a2ax402}, A402~\citep{a402}, or any future quote-and-pay protocol. The protocol stack provides addressing, settlement, and audit trails; 402Pilot supplies the buyer-side decision rule before settlement is invoked.

This boundary also fixes what 402Pilot does not attempt to solve. It does not implement on-chain settlement, capability discovery, production observability, or multi-agent coordination; those concerns belong to the protocol, registry, operator, or higher-level orchestration layers. We study a single-agent setting with one wallet, one policy, and one workload stream, and we leave online safeguards such as explicit drift detection and trust scoring to future work (\S\ref{sec:discuss}).

The boundary relies on three assumptions about the surrounding stack. First, the protocol can quote a price before a paid call is made, allowing the wallet to compute affordability and the policy to reason about cost. Second, outcomes are observable and scoreable shortly after the call, so the selected provider can be updated before the next round; in our benchmark, this is implemented with deterministic task evaluators or cached LLM-as-judge scores. Third, providers are non-strategic in the mechanism-design sense: they post prices and deliver services rather than bidding strategically for allocation. These assumptions match the x402-style quote-and-pay flow we instantiate and are compatible with the protocol peers discussed above. Strategic providers, as studied in recent mechanism-design work~\citep{patra2026reverseauction}, are complementary to this layer rather than a substitute for it.

Our reference implementation is a Python package (\texttt{pilot402}) of roughly $4{,}900$ source lines. The policy interface, wallet, context encoder, reward calculator, and per-seed runtime loop are separate modules, so each can be replaced without changing the others. Replays are deterministic given the seed and the frozen pre-generated dataset; the full main sweep ($5$ baselines $\times$ $3$ scenarios $\times$ $30$ seeds $\times$ $10{,}000$ rounds) finishes within a few CPU-hours on a single laptop, with no GPU dependency at run time because all LLM responses are cached.
