\section{The 402Pilot Decision Layer}
\label{sec:layer}

402Pilot turns the formal setting in \S\ref{sec:problem} into a narrow boundary between an autonomous agent and the payment substrate. Each round follows a quote--select--pay--observe loop: the layer receives a task and wallet snapshot, asks which providers are feasible, calls a policy to choose one of them, delegates the selected payment and service call, and returns only the chosen outcome to the policy. Feasible-set masking prevents unaffordable actions, and chosen-only updates keep the policy within the admissible information pattern of \S\ref{sec:problem}. The layer does not modify the protocol, hold funds, or cross the settlement boundary. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} sketches the per-round flow.

On the payment side, 402Pilot asks the substrate for three things:
\begin{align*}
&\mathtt{feasible}(\{p_{a,t}\}) \to \mathcal{A}_t,\qquad
\mathtt{pressure}() \to \lambda_t,\\
&\mathtt{pay}(a_t) \to (\mathrm{response}_t,\mathrm{receipt}_t).
\end{align*}
The receipt supplies the realized charge $c_t$, and a task scorer maps the response to $(q_t,f_t)$. In our frozen-replay benchmark, 402Pilot-Bench, both are read from cached evaluation records. No affordable action stops the run; paid non-delivery is feedback with $f_t=1$ and realized charge $c_t$. Retry, settlement faults, discovery, and observability remain outside the layer.

On the policy side, any selector exposes two methods:
\begin{align*}
&\mathtt{select}(\mathbf{x}_t,\, \mathcal{A}_t) \to a_t \in \mathcal{A}_t, \\
&\mathtt{update}(\mathbf{x}_t,\, a_t,\, \ut,\, c_t) \to \emptyset,
\end{align*}
where $\mathcal{A}_t \subseteq \{1, \ldots, K\}$ is the wallet-feasible subset at round $t$. The layer supplies a task-context representation $\mathbf{x}_t$; policies may use it directly or map it to internal buckets. Any implementation of these two methods is a drop-in policy, which is why fixed rules, random baselines, and learned policies share the same evaluation loop. Numeric effective charges are not passed to the policy before payment. Payment-aware policies may hold a read-only wallet handle scoped to selection-time budget pressure; non-payment-aware comparators ignore this channel. Priors, posteriors, and other internal state remain private to the policy.

A key design choice is that the update receives \emph{utility} $\ut$ and realized cost $c_t$, rather than the payment-aware reward $\rt$. The same payment-aware form defines the selection score and evaluation objective, but realized $\rt$ is not an observation of provider quality. Updating posteriors on $\rt$ would entangle provider beliefs with the policy's own wallet trajectory, undermining the posterior interpretation used in \S\ref{sec:method}: the same provider would appear worse late in a budget-tight run than early in the run, even if its service behavior had not changed. In 402Pilot, beliefs about provider utility and cost are updated from $(\ut,c_t)$, while wallet pressure enters selection through $\lambda_t$.

This boundary is also payment-rail agnostic: any rail that exposes payable candidates, executes the selected payment, and returns a charge receipt can sit underneath it. Settlement, provider discovery, observability, multi-agent coordination, and strategic bidding remain outside the layer. \S\ref{sec:method} instantiates the policy side with PA-DCT, a concrete method that uses the optional wallet-pressure channel while preserving the \texttt{select}/\texttt{update} contract.
