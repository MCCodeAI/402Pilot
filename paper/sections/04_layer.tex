\section{The 402Pilot Decision Layer}
\label{sec:layer}

402Pilot turns the formal setting in \S\ref{sec:problem} into a narrow boundary between an autonomous agent and the payment substrate. Each round follows a quote--select--pay--observe loop: the layer receives a task, wallet snapshot, and quoted candidate set, asks which providers are feasible, and calls a policy to choose one of them. The layer then delegates the selected payment and service call, and passes only the chosen outcome to the policy. Feasible-set masking prevents unaffordable actions, and chosen-only updates keep the policy within the admissible information pattern of \S\ref{sec:problem}. The layer does not modify the protocol, hold funds, or cross the settlement boundary; x402 is our motivating rail, but the same contract fits above any rail that exposes payable candidates, executes the selected payment, and returns a charge receipt. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} sketches the per-round flow.

On the payment side, 402Pilot consumes a quoted candidate set $\mathcal{Q}_t$: provider identifiers and prices produced upstream by discovery and pricing machinery, with the price component instantiating $\mathbf{p}_t$ from \S\ref{sec:problem}. Given $\mathcal{Q}_t$, the wallet/payment interface exposes three operations:
\begin{align*}
&\mathtt{feasible}(\mathcal{Q}_t) \to \mathcal{A}_t,\qquad
\mathtt{pressure}() \to \lambda_t,\\
&\mathtt{pay}(a_t) \to (\mathrm{response}_t,\mathrm{receipt}_t).
\end{align*}
The receipt supplies the realized charge $c_t$, and a task scorer maps the response to $(q_t,f_t)$. In our frozen-replay benchmark, 402Pilot-Bench, both are read from cached evaluation records. No affordable action stops the run; paid non-delivery is feedback with $f_t=1$ and realized charge $c_t$. Discovery, quote formation, retry, settlement faults, and observability remain outside the layer.

On the policy side, a policy exposes two methods:
\begin{align*}
&\mathtt{select}(\mathbf{x}_t,\, \mathcal{A}_t) \to a_t \in \mathcal{A}_t, \\
&\mathtt{update}(\mathbf{x}_t,\, a_t,\, \ut,\, c_t) \to \emptyset,
\end{align*}
where $\mathcal{A}_t \subseteq \{1, \ldots, K\}$ is the wallet-feasible subset at round $t$. The layer supplies a task-context representation $\mathbf{x}_t$; policies may use it directly or map it to internal buckets. Any implementation of these two methods is a drop-in policy---fixed rules, random baselines, and learned policies share the same evaluation loop. Two things stay outside the method interface: numeric effective charges before payment (the policy sees only $\mathcal{A}_t$), and policy internals (priors, posteriors, and other private state). Payment-aware policies may additionally read $\lambda_t$ through the optional wallet-pressure channel inside \texttt{select}; non-payment-aware comparators do not.

After observation, the layer converts scorer outputs into utility, $\ut=q_t-\nu f_t$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:utility}), and passes only $(\ut,c_t)$ to \texttt{update}. It does not pass the realized payment-aware reward $\rt$. That reward is useful for scoring decisions, but it mixes provider behavior with wallet pressure: the same provider could look worse simply because the wallet is tighter, not because its service quality changed. 402Pilot therefore learns provider utility and cost from $(\ut,c_t)$, and uses wallet pressure $\lambda_t$ only inside \texttt{select}.

The consequence is that payment execution becomes a learning interface: the substrate constrains what can be bought, and the policy learns what is worth buying. \S\ref{sec:method} instantiates that policy with PA-DCT, which uses wallet pressure during selection while preserving the same \texttt{select}/\texttt{update} contract.
