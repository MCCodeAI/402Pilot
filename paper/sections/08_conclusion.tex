\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Programmable settlement makes it possible for agents to pay, but it does not decide how an agent should spend a finite wallet. This paper formalized that missing problem as \emph{agent-native payment decision-making}: buyer-side provider selection under uncertain quality, changing reliability or prices, task context, and budget pressure. 402Pilot separates this decision from payment execution, and PA-DCT gives a concrete policy that learns from both service outcomes and realized costs.

On 402Pilot-Bench, PA-DCT trades stationary-regime performance for adaptation capacity: it remains budget-feasible while improving service quality during an outage, captures a price-promotion opportunity without provider-specific rules, and exposes three roles for its components--solvency (payment-awareness), shock adaptation (discounting and the learned cost posterior), and exploration stability (contextual bucketing and Thompson sampling). Across the nine rank-comparable evaluation cells, PA-DCT is the only non-oracle policy whose worst rank stays in the top half of the eight non-oracle entries, supporting a single adaptive buyer policy rather than scenario-specific rules. The broader conclusion is that autonomous payment systems need learning and evaluation at the buyer layer, not only safer settlement mechanisms. We release the code, frozen replay data, scenario sweeps, and evaluation harness to make this decision layer testable and extensible.
